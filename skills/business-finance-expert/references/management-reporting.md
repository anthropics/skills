# Management Reporting

## KPI Dashboard Design Principles

### The 10-Second Rule
If a stakeholder can't grasp the main takeaway within 10 seconds, the dashboard needs redesign. Clarity trumps comprehensiveness.

### Dashboard Architecture
- **Level 1 (CEO/Board)**: 5–8 high-level KPIs — ARR, Growth Rate, NRR, Gross Margin, Burn/Runway, Rule of 40/Burn Multiple
- **Level 2 (Functional leaders)**: Department-specific operational metrics tied to level 1 KPIs
- **Level 3 (Individual contributors)**: Daily/weekly operational data

### CFO Dashboard: Required Metrics
**Revenue & Growth:**
- ARR (current) and trailing 12-month growth rate
- MRR bridge (new, expansion, contraction, churn) — month over month
- Bookings (ACV) vs. plan
- Pipeline coverage (3x pipeline to quota is standard)

**Unit Economics:**
- NRR (trailing 12 months for each cohort)
- Gross margin (subscription vs. services separately)
- LTV:CAC ratio and trend
- CAC payback period by segment

**Efficiency:**
- Rule of 40 score (or Rule of X for investor-facing)
- Burn multiple (net burn / net new ARR)
- Magic number (ARR generated per S&M dollar)
- Headcount by department vs. plan

**Cash & Liquidity:**
- Cash balance and weekly trend
- Net burn rate (3-month average)
- Runway (months)
- AR aging and DSO

**Design best practices:**
- Place most critical metrics top-left (eye-tracking pattern)
- Use color consistently: green = on/above target, yellow = within 10% of target, red = >10% below
- Show trend lines, not just point-in-time values
- Link dashboards directly to source data (avoid manual updates)

## Board Reporting Package

### Standard Package Structure (monthly or quarterly)
1. **Executive summary / narrative (1 page)**: Key achievements, misses, and decisions needed
2. **Financial highlights**: P&L vs. budget/forecast, balance sheet, cash flow
3. **ARR / Revenue metrics**: Bridge, waterfall, cohort performance
4. **Operating metrics**: Department KPIs vs. plan
5. **Headcount**: Actuals vs. plan by department, open roles
6. **Forecast**: Updated QE and YE view with confidence interval
7. **Risks and opportunities**: Identified risks with probability/impact, mitigating actions

### Board Deck Financial Section: Best Practices
- Lead with the headline number (ARR or revenue) and growth rate
- Show trend (at least 6–8 quarters) not just current period
- Compare to budget AND to prior period (both matter)
- Add commentary explaining any variance >5%: was it timing, structural, or one-time?
- Include a "path to next milestone" slide: what needs to be true to hit ARR target / reach profitability?
- Avoid burying problems in footnotes — boards respect proactive transparency

### Financial Narrative Writing
Good financial narrative answers: What happened? Why? What are we doing about it?

**Template structure:**
> "Revenue was $X in Q[Y], [above/below] plan by $Z. The [beat/miss] was driven by [specific factor]: [explanation]. Looking ahead, [forward-looking context]. Our updated forecast for Q[Y+1] is $X, reflecting [assumption]."

Key rules:
- Quantify everything (not "slightly above" — say "4% above plan")
- Attribute variances to specific causes (not "macro headwinds")
- Connect metric to action: "NRR declined from 115% to 108% driven by SMB churn; we have implemented [specific actions] targeting a recovery to 112% by Q4"
- Keep it concise: boards don't read paragraphs; use headers, bullets, and bold key numbers

## Investor Updates

### Frequency and Format
- **Monthly updates** (common for early-stage VC-backed companies): 500–800 words, email format
- **Quarterly updates** (Series B+): More formal; often a deck
- **Annual letter**: Full-year summary with strategic context

### Monthly Investor Update Template
**Header metrics**: MRR/ARR, MRR growth (MoM and YoY), Gross Margin, Burn Rate, Cash Runway

**Section 1 — Highlights (2–3 bullets):**
- Key wins: new customer logo, partnership signed, product launch, team hire
- Metrics beating plan

**Section 2 — Lowlights / Challenges (1–2 bullets):**
- Be honest; investors respect transparency and will know eventually
- What is being done to address?

**Section 3 — Key Metrics (table or simple chart):**
MRR, New MRR, Expansion MRR, Churned MRR, NRR, Cash, Burn Rate, Headcount

**Section 4 — Asks:**
- Specific requests: introductions, references, candidates, partnerships
- Investors who don't get asks stop engaging

**Best practices:**
- Send on a consistent schedule (same day each month)
- Keep it short — investors read dozens of updates; < 500 words for monthly
- Celebrate wins and acknowledge misses in the same communication
- Show progress against milestones you set in the prior update

## Due Diligence Financial Preparation

When preparing for a fundraise or acquisition, prepare the financial data room in advance:

### Core Financial Documents
- 3–5 years of audited (or reviewed) financial statements
- Monthly P&L for the last 24 months
- ARR/MRR bridge going back 24+ months
- Customer-level ARR data (with NDA protections in place)
- NRR cohort analysis (by year of acquisition)
- Unit economics model: CAC, LTV, payback period by channel and segment
- Revenue recognition policy document (and any 606 memo)
- Cap table (fully diluted)

### Financial Metrics Benchmarking
Benchmark your metrics against public SaaS companies and venture/growth stage peers:
- ARR growth rate: compare to Rule of 40 threshold and T2D3 trajectory
- Gross margin: target 70%+ for subscription; note services blended margin separately
- NRR: >110% is strong for institutional investors; >120% for premium valuation
- Burn multiple: <2x is "good" for VC; <1.5x for Series A in 2024+ environment
- LTV:CAC: >3x; document assumptions clearly

**Data sources for benchmarking:**
- Bessemer Venture Partners State of the Cloud (annual)
- OpenView Partners SaaS Benchmarks Survey
- Benchmarkit (KeyBanc) annual SaaS survey
- SaaS Capital Index (private company benchmarks)
- Battery Ventures Metrics that Matter
