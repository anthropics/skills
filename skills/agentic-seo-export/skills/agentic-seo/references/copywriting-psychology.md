# Copywriting & Marketing Psychology

## The Hierarchy of Copy

Every piece of copy must answer in order:
1. **Is this for me?** (Audience identification)
2. **What is it?** (Clarity)
3. **Why does it matter?** (Relevance)
4. **Why should I believe you?** (Credibility)
5. **What do I do next?** (CTA)

Fail at #1 or #2 and nothing else matters.

## Copywriting Frameworks

### PAS (Problem → Agitate → Solution)
Best for: landing pages, ads, email subject lines

```
Problem:  "Your ads are burning money on the wrong keywords."
Agitate:  "Every day without keyword intelligence, you're handing leads to competitors 
           who show up in searches you don't even know about."
Solution: "Semrush's PPC Keyword Tool finds the exact terms driving conversions in your 
           market — before you spend a dollar."
```

### AIDA (Attention → Interest → Desire → Action)
Best for: long-form sales pages, email sequences

```
Attention: Bold headline, stat, or contrarian claim
Interest:  Build context, introduce the problem space  
Desire:    Show the transformation, proof, benefits
Action:    Clear, specific CTA
```

### BAB (Before → After → Bridge)
Best for: testimonials, case studies, email

```
Before:  "Spent 6 hours/week manually tracking competitors."
After:   "Now I get automated alerts and weekly reports."
Bridge:  "Semrush EyeOn monitors everything and sends digests."
```

### 4 U's (Headlines)
Score your headline 1–4 on each:
- **Useful**: Does it offer value?
- **Urgent**: Does it create time pressure?
- **Unique**: Is it different from obvious?
- **Ultra-specific**: Does it use numbers/details?

Example: "How to Get 50% More Organic Traffic in 90 Days (Free Audit Checklist)"
- Useful ✅ Urgent ✅ Unique (50%/90 days) ✅ Ultra-specific ✅

## Marketing Psychology Principles

### 1. Social Proof
People follow what others do — especially peers.

**Types ranked by power:**
1. Named customer testimonials with photo + title + company
2. Case studies with specific numbers ("3× traffic in 60 days")
3. Review platform scores (G2, Capterra, Trustpilot)
4. Logo walls (recognizable brands)
5. User counts ("Trusted by 10 million marketers")

**Rules:**
- Specificity > vagueness ("increased revenue by 47%" beats "grew my business")
- Peer relevance matters (show reviews from your target persona)
- Recency signals credibility (show review dates)

### 2. Reciprocity
Give value before asking for something.

**Applications:**
- Free tools, templates, audits
- Valuable content before email gate
- Unexpected upgrade/gift at onboarding

### 3. Scarcity & Urgency
Only works when genuine.

**Real urgency:**
- Seat limits (webinars, cohorts)
- Beta access (genuinely limited)
- Price increase announcements
- Seasonal offers

**Fake urgency kills trust.** Never use countdown timers that reset.

### 4. Anchoring
First number seen becomes the reference point.

**Pricing application:**
- Show original price before discount
- Show enterprise/highest plan first
- Annual price shown monthly ("just $X/month")

### 5. Loss Aversion
People feel losses ~2× more than equivalent gains.

**Reframe gains as loss prevention:**
- "Stop losing leads to competitors" vs "Get more leads"
- "Don't miss this ranking opportunity" vs "Improve your rankings"
- "Every day you wait, competitors pull further ahead"

### 6. Cognitive Load Reduction
Fewer choices = more decisions. More choices = paralysis.

**Apply to:**
- Navigation: fewer top-level items
- Pricing: 3 plans max, highlight one
- CTAs: one primary per page section
- Forms: minimum fields

### 7. Specificity as Credibility
Specific claims feel more trustworthy than vague ones.

- "Improves rankings" → weak
- "Average 34% ranking improvement in 60 days" → strong
- "Most customers see results" → weak
- "87% of customers rank on page 1 within 90 days" → strong

## Ad Copywriting (`ad-creative`)

### Google Ads (RSA)
- 15 headlines (30 chars each)
- 4 descriptions (90 chars each)
- Google mixes them — write each as standalone

**Headline types to include:**
- Keyword headline: "SEO Tools for Agencies"
- Benefit headline: "Rank Higher, Grow Faster"
- Feature headline: "200+ SEO + Marketing Tools"
- Social proof: "Trusted by 10M+ Marketers"
- Urgency/offer: "Start Free 7-Day Trial"
- Question: "Why Are Competitors Outranking You?"

### Meta Ads
**Hook (first 3 seconds/125 chars):**
- Call out the audience: "Attention: e-commerce store owners…"
- Lead with the problem: "Tired of guessing which keywords work?"
- Lead with the result: "How we 3× organic traffic without paying for ads"

**Body copy structure:**
- Expand on hook
- Build desire (specific benefits/proof)
- Address objection
- CTA

**Creative principles:**
- Stop the scroll: bold colors, faces, motion
- Show don't tell: product screenshots > feature lists
- Native feel: looks like organic content, not an ad
- One clear message per ad (not everything at once)

## Email Copywriting (`email-sequence`, `cold-email`)

### Subject Line Formulas
- Question: "Are you making this keyword mistake?"
- Number: "5 SEO wins you can get this week"
- Curiosity gap: "The ranking factor most sites ignore"
- Personalization: "[Company] + one SEO fix = ?"
- Direct: "Your site audit results"

**Rules:**
- 40 chars or less (mobile preview)
- No clickbait (kills trust after first open)
- Test: curiosity vs direct vs benefit-led

### Cold Email Structure
```
Subject: [Specific + relevant, not salesy]

Line 1: Genuine observation about THEM (not you)
Line 2: Relevance bridge (why you're reaching out)
Line 3: Value proposition (one sentence)
Line 4: Single soft CTA ("Would it make sense to chat?")

[First name]
```

**Rules:**
- Under 100 words
- No attachments on first email
- One ask only
- Personal, not broadcast-feeling
- Research-backed opening (shows you did homework)

## Product Marketing Context (`product-marketing-context`)

Before writing any copy, define:

**Positioning:**
- Who is the primary customer? (job title, company size, pain)
- What is the category? (what alternatives do they consider?)
- What is the key differentiator? (why are you better for them specifically?)

**Messaging hierarchy:**
1. Primary message: one sentence that captures the whole value
2. Supporting proof points: 3-5 specific claims with evidence
3. Feature benefits: how features translate to outcomes

**Persona pain stack:**
- Functional pain: what task is broken/hard?
- Emotional pain: how does it make them feel?
- Social pain: how does it affect their reputation/career?

Copy that addresses all three pain levels outperforms feature-focused copy consistently.

## Competitive Positioning (`competitor-alternatives`)

**Alternative pages ("X alternatives" keywords):**
- High intent: user is actively researching
- Target: "[Competitor] alternatives" keywords with your tool as answer

**Structure:**
1. Empathize with the problem (why they're looking for alternatives)
2. Feature comparison table (honest, you don't need to win every row)
3. Use case matching (which tool is best for which user)
4. Your option positioned for specific use case
5. CTA tied to your differentiation

**Rule:** Don't trash competitors. Position honestly. Users researching alternatives are sophisticated — they'll see through it.
