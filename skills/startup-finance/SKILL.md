## 1. STARTUP FUNDING MECHANICS

### Round Structure & Current Check Sizes (2025-2026 Market)

**Pre-Seed**
- Amount: $500K–$2M (median $750K–$1.5M); hot AI teams in SF/NY can raise $2–3M
- Post-money valuation: $4–10M (median $5–8M)
- Dilution: 15–25%
- Instrument: SAFE (90%+ of deals in Q1 2025; 92% use SAFEs per Carta/a16z data)
- Lead: angels, pre-seed micro-VCs, founder friends
- Milestone required: idea + team; some MVP or early users

**Seed**
- Amount: $2.5M–$5.6M (median $3–3.8M in 2025; upper quartile $5.6M)
- Post-money valuation: $12–20M (median national $16M per Carta Q1 2025; CA closer to $17M)
- Dilution: 15–22%
- Instrument: SAFE (cap-only SAFEs = 81% of deals in Q1 2025, up from 76% in 2024) or priced round
- Lead check: $500K–$2M from a lead seed VC; 3–5 co-investors
- Milestone required: MVP, early traction, $100K–$1M ARR or strong user metrics

**Series A**
- Amount: $10–20M (median ~$12M)
- Pre-money valuation: $40–55M; post-money $50–75M
- Dilution: 18–22% (median fell to ~17.9% in Q1 2025 per Carta)
- Instrument: Priced preferred equity (always)
- Lead: institutional VC; one board seat
- Lead check: $5–12M
- Milestone required: product-market fit, $1–3M ARR, demonstrable growth rate (T2D3 or better)

**Series B**
- Amount: $25–60M (median ~$38M in Q3 2025)
- Pre-money valuation: $100–250M
- Dilution: 15–20%
- Milestone: $8–20M ARR, clear unit economics, repeatable GTM

**Series C+**
- Amount: $50M–$200M+
- Valuation: $300M–$1B+
- Milestone: Category leadership, $30M+ ARR, path to profitability

**Key Market Context (2025-2026):** Round sizes are 30–50% below 2021 peaks. Seed-to-Series A conversion has fallen from ~50% to ~38%. Only ~9% of seed-funded startups reach Series A within 3 years. Post-money SAFE structures (YC standard) dominate; pre-money SAFEs are largely obsolete.

### SAFEs vs. Convertible Notes vs. Priced Rounds

**SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity)**
- Not debt: no interest accrual, no maturity date
- Converts on triggering event: priced equity round, acquisition, IPO, or dissolution
- YC introduced post-money SAFE as standard (2018); this is now market standard
- Most common structure: valuation cap only (81% of deals Q1 2025)
- Cap + discount: dual protection, appears in deals where investor has leverage
- MFN (Most Favored Nation) clause: if future SAFEs have better terms (lower cap, higher discount), this SAFE automatically upgrades to match — important for early angels
- Pro-rata rights: right to participate in future rounds to maintain ownership; negotiate for SAFE amounts >$500K
- Discount rates: typically 15–20% (discount on Series A price if no cap, or better of cap vs. discount if both)
- Common caps in 2025: $8–15M for pre-seed; $15–25M for seed
- Use when: raising $50K–$2M from angels; uncertain valuation; want simplicity

**Convertible Notes**
- IS debt: accrues interest (typically 5–8% simple; rose 200–300 bps in 2025), has maturity date (12–24 months)
- Same conversion mechanics as SAFE but with debt obligations
- If the round doesn't happen by maturity: note holder can demand repayment OR convert at cap (negotiated)
- Interest rates on convertible notes climbed as high as 10.5pp in larger deals (2025)
- Use when: bridge capital between priced rounds; some institutional investors require debt structure; jurisdictions where SAFEs are not recognized
- Founder risk: maturity date creates pressure; can trigger default if fundraising stalls

**Priced Rounds**
- Sets a definitive valuation at time of investment
- Issues preferred stock with protective provisions: liquidation preference, anti-dilution, pro-rata rights, information rights, board seats
- More expensive to execute ($15K–$50K in legal fees vs. $2K–5K for SAFEs)
- Always required at Series A and beyond
- Key documents: term sheet, stock purchase agreement (SPA), investor rights agreement (IRA), right of first refusal and co-sale agreement (ROFR/Co-Sale), voting agreement, amended & restated certificate of incorporation

**Liquidation Preference (2025 Market Standard)**
- 1x non-participating: 98% of VC deals use 1x multiple (per Cooley Q2 2025); 95% non-participating
- Non-participating: investor gets GREATER of (1x investment back) OR (convert to common and share pro-rata) — not both
- Participating preferred: investor gets 1x back PLUS shares in remaining proceeds — largely fallen out of favor at top-tier VCs; appears in down rounds and corp VC deals
- Full ratchet anti-dilution: resets conversion price to new lower price in down round — avoid
- Broad-based weighted average (BBWA): market standard anti-dilution; moderate adjustment based on size of new issuance

## 2. VC VALUATION METHODS

### The VC Method (Bill Sahlman, 1987)
Works backward from exit, discounts to present:

1. **Estimate exit value**: Revenue or EBITDA at exit × exit multiple (e.g., $20M ARR × 8x = $160M)
2. **Apply target return**: VC needs 10x in 7-10 years → required post-money = $160M / 10 = $16M
3. **Calculate dilution from future rounds**: If 3 more rounds dilute by 50% total → adjust: $16M / (1 - 0.50) = $32M post-money now
4. **Determine pre-money**: Post-money minus investment amount → if raising $3M: Pre-money = $32M - $3M = $29M

**Important**: VCs are building portfolios where 70% of investments return <1x. They need outliers that return the entire fund. A $500M fund needs a $5B outcome to 10x (at 10% ownership). Your valuation must fit their portfolio math.

### Comparable Company Analysis (Comps)
- Find recent funding rounds of similar companies (same stage, sector, geography, metrics)
- Apply revenue multiples: e.g., if comparable seed SaaS companies trade at 10–15x ARR, and you have $500K ARR, your comps suggest $5–7.5M value
- Layer in growth rate premium/discount: growing 300% YoY commands premium; declining growth warrants haircut
- Sources: Carta data, PitchBook, Crunchbase, Visible VC

### DCF for Startups
- Rarely used pre-revenue due to sensitivity to terminal value assumptions
- More useful at Series B+ when revenue is predictable
- Apply 30–50% discount rates (reflecting startup risk) vs. 8–12% for public companies
- Terminal value typically dominates (80–90% of DCF value) — small assumption changes = enormous value swings

### Pre-Money vs. Post-Money Math
- Post-money = Pre-money + Investment
- Investor ownership = Investment / Post-money
- Example: $3M raised on $12M pre-money → $15M post-money → investor owns 20%
- Founder ownership after: if founders had 100% → now 80%
- Key nuance with SAFEs: SAFE valuation caps set the MAXIMUM price per share at conversion, not the current valuation

### The Option Pool Shuffle — Critical Founder Trap
When investors require an option pool created PRE-MONEY (happens in ~90-95% of deals):

**Example showing the trap:**
- Negotiated pre-money: $10M
- Investment: $2M → expected post-money: $12M → investor gets 16.7%
- Investor requires 15% post-money option pool (created pre-money)
- 15% of $12M = $1.8M worth of options created BEFORE investment
- Effective pre-money = $10M - $1.8M = $8.2M
- Founders now own: $8.2M / $12M = 68.3% (not 83.3% as assumed)
- INVESTOR still gets 16.7%; OPTION POOL takes 15%; FOUNDERS bear all dilution

**How to fight it:**
- Request option pool post-money (rare but possible with leverage)
- Build a realistic 18-month hiring plan to justify smaller pool (10% instead of 20%)
- Negotiate the pool size down: every 1% reduction = real founder equity preserved
- Typical post-Series A ESOP pool: 10–15%; post-seed: 10–12%

### Founder Ownership Benchmarks (Carta 2025 data)
- After seed: median founding team retains ~56.2%
- After Series A: ~36.1%
- After Series B: ~23%
- Typical VC ownership at various stages: Seed VC ~20%, Series A VC ~18-22%, Series B VC ~15-20%

## 3. FINANCIAL MODELING FOR TECH STARTUPS

### SaaS Metrics — The Complete Framework

**Core Revenue Metrics**
- **MRR (Monthly Recurring Revenue)**: Total contracted monthly recurring revenue. Exclude one-time fees, professional services, usage that may vary
- **ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue)**: MRR × 12 (NOT last 12 months of revenue — that's trailing revenue)
- **ACV (Annual Contract Value)**: Average annual value per customer contract
- **TCV (Total Contract Value)**: Full contract value including multi-year deals

**Growth Metrics**
- **New MRR**: Revenue from new customers added this month
- **Expansion MRR**: Additional revenue from existing customers (upsells, upgrades, seat expansion)
- **Contraction MRR**: Revenue lost from downgrades
- **Churned MRR**: Revenue lost from cancellations
- **Net New MRR** = New MRR + Expansion MRR - Contraction MRR - Churned MRR

**Retention Metrics (Most Important for Investors)**
- **Gross Revenue Retention (GRR)**: (Beginning MRR - Contraction - Churn) / Beginning MRR. Ceiling is 100%. Measures downside protection. Benchmark: >85% for SMB, >90% for Enterprise
- **Net Revenue Retention (NRR)**: (Beginning MRR + Expansion - Contraction - Churn) / Beginning MRR. Can exceed 100%. The single most important investor metric in 2025.
  - Formula: NRR = (Start MRR + Expansion - Contraction - Churn) / Start MRR × 100
  - World-class: >120% (Snowflake peaked at 170%+)
  - Excellent: 110–120% (enterprise); 100–110% (SMB)
  - Acceptable: >100% (installed base grows without new sales)
  - Warning: <100% (treadmill — must acquire new customers just to maintain revenue)
  - 2025 benchmark medians: NRR 104–106% for top performers
- **Logo Churn**: % of customers canceling. Benchmark: <5% annually for enterprise, <10% for SMB
- **Cohort Analysis**: Group customers by signup month; track their revenue over time. Reveals if newer cohorts are better/worse than older ones. Essential for investor diligence

**Unit Economics**
- **CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)**: (Sales + Marketing spend) / New customers acquired
  - Fully-loaded CAC includes: salesperson OTE (on-target earnings), marketing spend, tools, allocated overhead
  - Payback period = CAC / (Monthly Revenue per customer × Gross Margin %)
  - Benchmark payback: <12 months (scale), 12–18 months (healthy growth), 18–24 months (early stage OK), >24 months (unsustainable without clear path to improvement)
  - 2025 median CAC payback: ~20 months across SaaS sector
- **LTV (Customer Lifetime Value)**: ARPU × Gross Margin % × (1 / Churn Rate)
  - Example: $500/month ARPU × 75% GM × (1 / 10% annual churn) = $500 × 0.75 × 10 = $3,750
  - LTV:CAC ratio benchmark: >3:1 minimum, 5:1+ excellent, >10:1 may mean underinvesting in growth
- **Gross Margin**: (Revenue - COGS) / Revenue
  - COGS for SaaS: hosting/AWS, customer success (if dedicated), payment processing, third-party API costs
  - Benchmark: 70–85% for traditional SaaS; 25–60% for AI-native (see AI section below)

**Efficiency Metrics**
- **Rule of 40**: Revenue Growth Rate % + EBITDA/FCF Margin % ≥ 40%
  - Companies consistently above 40 command 20–30% premium on ARR multiples
  - Only 11–30% of SaaS companies hit this benchmark
  - IPO-readiness benchmark (2025): Rule of 40 score >40, $100M+ ARR, >30% YoY growth, NRR >115%, GM >72%
- **Burn Multiple**: Net Burn / Net New ARR (Bessemer Venture Partners metric)
  - Measures how much you burn per dollar of new ARR
  - <1x: Outstanding (adding more ARR than you're burning)
  - 1–1.5x: Good
  - 1.5–2x: Acceptable early stage
  - >2x: Concern; >3x: Unsustainable
  - Investor expectation 2026: burn multiple <1.0x at scale; <2.0x at early stage
- **Bessemer Efficiency Score (BES)**: Net New ARR / Net Burn (inverse of Burn Multiple; higher is better)
- **Magic Number**: Net New ARR (annualized) / Prior Quarter S&M Spend. >0.75 = efficient GTM; <0.5 = inefficient

**Cohort Analysis Best Practices**
- Track each monthly cohort's MRR from month 0 (signup) through 12, 24, 36 months
- Look for "smiling curves" — revenue that dips then recovers as expansion kicks in
- Compare cohort curves: are newer cohorts performing better or worse at month 6, 12?
- Calculate LTV by cohort, not just as a blended average
- Present to investors as heat maps with months on X-axis, cohort start months on Y-axis

### Financial Model Architecture

**Three-Statement Model**
1. **Income Statement (P&L)**: Revenue → Gross Profit → Operating Expenses → EBITDA → Net Income
2. **Balance Sheet**: Assets (cash, AR, etc.) = Liabilities (AP, deferred revenue, debt) + Equity
3. **Cash Flow Statement**: Operating Cash Flow + Investing Cash Flow + Financing Cash Flow

**SaaS Revenue Model Structure**
- Start with customer cohorts by acquisition month
- Model: New MRR added each month (from sales capacity × conversion × ACV)
- Apply monthly churn to existing cohort
- Apply expansion revenue (NRR-implied growth on retained base)
- Roll forward to get total MRR; × 12 for ARR

**Scenario Planning (Required for Investor Models)**
- Base case: realistic assumptions
- Bull case: top quartile growth, improving CAC, expansion revenue kicks in
- Bear case: growth 50% below plan, churn increases, fundraising delayed 3 months
- Key sensitivities to model: churn rate (±2%), CAC (±25%), growth rate (±30%), hiring pace (±20%)

**Model Best Practices (2025 Investor Standards)**
- Monthly granularity for 18–24 months; quarterly thereafter (5 years total)
- Every assumption should be on a separate "assumptions" tab — not hard-coded in formulas
- Revenue should be derivable from first principles (pipeline × close rate × ACV × time-to-close)
- Headcount plan drives most of OpEx — build a detailed hiring plan by role, start date, salary, benefits loaded
- Defer revenue properly for annual contracts paid upfront (GAAP requirement)
- Bottom-up AND top-down validation: build from unit economics up, then sanity check against market size
- Update monthly for internal use; quarterly for board/investors

## 4. BURN RATE & RUNWAY MANAGEMENT

### Formulas
- **Gross Burn** = Total monthly cash outflows (all expenses before revenue)
- **Net Burn** = Gross Burn - Revenue received (on a cash basis)
- **Runway** = Cash on Hand / Net Monthly Burn
- Example: $3M cash, $200K/month net burn → 15 months runway

### Runway Decision Framework
| Runway Remaining | Action |
|------------------|--------|
| >18 months | Execute; begin building relationships with next-round investors |
| 12–18 months | Begin fundraising process immediately; identify top 20 target investors |
| 8–12 months | Urgent fundraise mode OR aggressive cost cuts to extend runway |
| <6 months | Crisis; accept worse terms; cut costs first, then fundraise |

**Key insight from market data**: Startups raising with <6 months runway accept valuations 35–50% lower on average than those raising with 12+ months. Never let yourself get desperate.

### The 18-Month Rule (2025 Standard)
- Investors expect you to demonstrate 18–24 months of post-funding runway
- Burn multiple benchmark 2026: <1.0x for companies at scale; <2.0x early stage
- "Grow at all costs" era is definitively over — capital efficiency is baseline expectation

### Strategies to Extend Runway
1. **Revenue acceleration** (highest ROI): Adding $10K MRR = same effect as cutting $10K/month in costs, but compounds
2. **Annual prepay incentives**: Offer 15–20% discount for annual vs. monthly — instant cash infusion
3. **Headcount freeze/defer**: Headcount = 60–80% of startup expenses; every unfilled role at $12K/month base = $12K/month burn saved
4. **Subscription audit**: Average startup wastes 15–25% of budget on redundant SaaS tools
5. **Vendor renegotiation**: AWS credits (startups get $100K–$500K); negotiate payment terms (net-60 instead of net-30)
6. **Revenue-based financing**: Non-dilutive; Founderpath, Lighter Capital advance against ARR at 1.5–2.5x monthly revenue, repay as % of revenue
7. **Venture debt**: See Section 7 for details on timing and risks
8. **Government grants**: See Section 9 for SBIR/STTR and non-dilutive sources
9. **Bridge round**: Convertible note from existing investors at 15–20% discount to next round; buys 6–12 months

### When to Cut vs. Raise
- Cut first if: burn multiple >2x, growth <20% YoY, unit economics not proven
- Raise first if: strong growth, proven unit economics, market window is open
- Both simultaneously: cut waste (never cut growth drivers), then raise with improved metrics
- Raising prices: often more runway-effective than cost cuts; 10% price increase = 10% GM improvement with no burn impact

## 5. CAP TABLE MANAGEMENT

### Equity Split Best Practices

**Founder Splits**
- 50/50 is mathematically clean but requires honest alignment conversation
- Splits based on role contribution: CEO often 40–60%, technical co-founder 30–50%
- Vesting: 4-year vest, 1-year cliff is market standard; always use vesting (protects against early departures)
- Acceleration: double trigger preferred (acceleration only on acquisition + termination); single trigger is red flag to investors

**Investor Equity (Typical Dilution by Round)**
- Pre-seed: 15–25%
- Seed: 15–22%
- Series A: 18–22%
- Series B: 15–20%
- ESOP pool: 10–20% (created/expanded each round, allocated to employees over time)

**409A Valuations**
- Required every 12 months OR within 90 days of a new funding round (whichever is sooner)
- Sets the "fair market value" of common stock for strike price of stock options
- Must be defensible to IRS — independent third-party required for safe harbor protection
- Common stock is typically 20–33% of preferred stock price (the "common-to-preferred discount")
- Pulley: 409A delivered in 5 days; Carta: varies, can be slower
- Cost: $500–$3,000 for standard 409A; $3K–8K for complex situations
- Timing: get 409A BEFORE issuing options; issuing options without current 409A = tax risk for employees (Section 409A penalty = 20% excise tax + interest)

**Cap Table Tools (2025 Comparison)**
- **Carta**: Most widely used; comprehensive features; free plan for <25 stakeholders; costs increase dramatically at scale ($5K–$25K/year for funded startups); recent fee controversies; best for complex equity events
- **Pulley**: More founder-friendly pricing; fixed per-stakeholder cost; 409A in 5 days; integrates with Mercury for wire transfers and SAFE issuance; strong alternative to Carta
- **Capshare**: Lower cost, simpler interface, good for early stage
- **Spreadsheet (pre-seed only)**: Acceptable until first external investors; must transition to software before Seed close
- **Stripe Atlas**: Includes founder equity setup, EIN, 83(b) election filing — good for incorporation but hand off cap table to Carta/Pulley after

**ESOP/Stock Option Pool**
- ISOs (Incentive Stock Options): For employees; favorable tax treatment if exercised and held; limited to $100K/year vesting at exercise price
- NSOs (Non-Qualified Stock Options): For contractors, advisors, non-US employees; less favorable tax treatment
- Early exercise: Allow employees to exercise unvested options (83(b) election opportunity); converts options to restricted stock with clock started on capital gains holding period
- Strike price = 409A FMV at time of grant; lower is better for employees
- Standard vesting: 4 years, 1-year cliff, monthly vesting thereafter

## 6. REVENUE MODELS FOR TECH STARTUPS

### SaaS (Subscription)
- Per seat/user: Predictable, scales with customer growth; examples: Salesforce, Zoom
- Tiered plans: Starter/Pro/Enterprise tiers; captures different willingness to pay; most common B2B model
- Hybrid (subscription + usage): Base subscription + variable component; best of both worlds; examples: AWS, Twilio
- **Best for**: Products where value correlates with number of users or feature access

### Usage-Based / Consumption
- Customers pay per API call, compute hour, GB processed, email sent
- Removes barrier to entry; captures upside from heavy users
- Creates revenue volatility (harder to model ARR); requires careful unit economics tracking
- Examples: Stripe (% of transaction), Snowflake (compute credits), OpenAI (per token)
- **Best for**: Infrastructure, APIs, AI services, products with highly variable usage

### Marketplace
- Take rate on GMV (Gross Merchandise Value): typically 5–30% depending on sector
- Two-sided network effects create defensibility; hard to build, hard to displace
- Unit economics: compare CAC for supply vs. demand side; track GMV, take rate, net revenue
- Examples: Airbnb (~15% take rate), Stripe (2.9% + $0.30), Uber (~25–30%)

### Freemium
- Free tier drives top-of-funnel; convert to paid on value threshold
- Conversion benchmarks: 2–5% free-to-paid (consumer), 5–15% (PLG B2B)
- Viral coefficient: does free user invite others? PLG (product-led growth) requires viral loop
- Free tier must be valuable enough to attract users but limited enough to drive upgrade
- Key metric: time to value (how fast does user hit paywall/need to upgrade?)

### Enterprise / Custom Pricing
- ACV typically $50K–$500K+; sold through outbound sales motion
- NDA-protected pricing; requires dedicated CSM (customer success manager)
- Longer sales cycles (3–12 months); higher CAC but also higher LTV and lower churn
- Champions, economic buyers, legal/security/procurement as stakeholders in enterprise deals

### Pricing Strategy
- **Value-based pricing**: Price based on value delivered (% of savings created, % of revenue generated) — highest ceiling, hardest to implement
- **Competitive pricing**: Price at or near competitors; defender position
- **Cost-plus pricing**: Cost of delivery + margin — floor of pricing; use for sanity check only
- **Land and expand**: Low initial ACV ($10K–$25K), expand within account to $100K+ over 2–3 years
- **Pricing rule of thumb**: 10x price increase attempts often yield 3–4x revenue; find inflection point where churn spikes vs. revenue gain

## 7. FUNDRAISING STRATEGY

### Pitch Deck Structure

**Sequoia Format (11-13 slides)**
1. Company Purpose — one sentence defining the company
2. Problem — the pain; quantify the cost of the problem
3. Solution — your product; show don't tell; demo if possible
4. Why Now — market timing, technology shift, regulatory change that makes this the moment
5. Market Size — TAM / SAM / SOM (bottom-up preferred; investors distrust top-down)
6. Competition — honest landscape; define your winning differentiation (not "no competition")
7. Product — deeper dive; screenshots, demo, technical differentiation
8. Business Model — how you make money; unit economics
9. Traction — the most important slide; ARR, growth rate, key customers, retention
10. Team — why you? Relevant experience, domain expertise, unfair advantages
11. Financials — 18–24 month projections, burn rate, runway, use of funds

**YC Format (Seed)**
- 10–15 slides maximum; minimalist; narrative as "fundraising vertebrae" (bullet points → slides)
- Problem + Solution + Why Now + Market + Team + Traction + Ask
- Metrics over stories; show, don't tell
- One killer insight per slide; instant comprehension

**Traction Metrics by Stage (What Investors Actually Want)**
- Pre-seed: team + idea + early validation (waitlist, LOIs, beta users)
- Seed: $100K–$1M ARR, or 10–50K MAU with clear monetization path; MoM growth 15–25%
- Series A: $1–3M ARR, NRR >100%, CAC payback <18 months, clear GTM repeatability
- Series B: $10M+ ARR, proven GTM playbook, clear path to profitability at scale

### Investor Outreach
- Warm introductions convert 5–10x better than cold outreach; build your investor CRM
- Target investors by: stage fit (they actually invest at your stage), sector focus, check size, geography
- Run a tight process: 30–45 day fundraising sprint; create urgency
- Lead with your strongest metric in first sentence of outreach email
- Use tools: Signal (YC's investor database), Crunchbase, Airtable/Notion for tracking
- Goal: 3–5 term sheets; competition drives better terms

### Due Diligence Preparation (Data Room)
Standard data room contents:
- Cap table (Carta/Pulley export)
- Corporate documents (certificate of incorporation, bylaws, shareholder agreements)
- Financial model (3-year projections + actuals to date)
- MRR/ARR schedule with cohort retention data
- Key customer contracts (redacted)
- Signed customer letters of intent or reference contacts
- IP assignments from all founders and employees
- 409A valuation report
- Any prior term sheets or convertible notes
- Employment agreements for key personnel
- Technical architecture overview

### Term Sheet Negotiation — Key Terms to Understand

**Economic terms** (most important):
- Pre-money valuation and investment amount
- Option pool creation timing (pre vs. post-money — see Section 2)
- Liquidation preference (1x non-participating is standard)
- Anti-dilution protection (BBWA is standard)

**Control terms**:
- Board composition (aim for founder-majority or 2:2:1 structure)
- Protective provisions (investor veto rights on major decisions)
- Information rights (quarterly financials, annual audit)
- Pro-rata rights (investor right to participate in future rounds)

**Traps and Red Flags:**
- Participating preferred >1x: gives investors double-dip on exit
- Full ratchet anti-dilution: punishes founders severely in down rounds
- High liquidation multiple (2x, 3x): investor gets 2–3x money back before founders see anything
- Board control provisions: investor gets >50% board control at seed is unusual
- Pay-to-play: investors who don't participate in future rounds get punished (this can actually be founder-friendly in some structures)
- Cram-down provisions: allows investors to dilute non-participating founders
- No-shop > 45 days: too long; creates opportunity cost; 30 days is market standard

### Venture Debt
- Non-dilutive capital secured against company's future revenue or assets
- Typically 6–36 months repayment; interest 8–15% annually; includes warrants (0.5–1% of round)
- Good use: extend runway post-equity round, finance equipment, bridge to revenue milestone
- Bad use: substitute for revenue or equity when business fundamentals are weak
- Market 2025: US venture debt at record $53.3B in 2024 (up 94.5% from 2023); but deal count lowest in decade (lenders more selective)
- Providers post-SVB: Hercules Capital, TriplePoint, Trinity Capital, Espresso Capital, JPMorgan, re-formed SVB (now First Citizens division)
- Red flags in venture debt term sheets: interest >18%, origination fees >3%, covenants requiring approval for routine decisions, prepayment penalties >3%, vague "material adverse change" definitions
- Rule: only take venture debt when you have 12+ months equity runway — don't use it as desperation capital

## 8. AI-SPECIFIC STARTUP FINANCE

### How AI Startups Are Fundamentally Different

**Cost Structure**
- AI-native cost of goods sold (COGS) includes: inference costs, GPU clusters, vector database costs, third-party API fees (OpenAI, Anthropic, etc.), model training and fine-tuning costs
- GPU and compute costs = 30–50% of revenue for most AI startups (vs. near-zero marginal cost for traditional SaaS)
- 84% of AI companies see 6%+ gross margin erosion from AI infrastructure costs
- This changes LTV calculations, unit economics, and the entire margin expansion story

**Gross Margin Reality (Bessemer "Supernovas" 2025)**
- Bessemer identifies two AI archetypes:
  - **Supernovas**: ~25% gross margins with unoptimized infrastructure; trading distribution for short-term profit; grow incredibly fast ($40M ARR in year 1, $125M ARR in year 2)
  - **Shooting Stars**: 60%+ gross margins after custom models, optimized infrastructure, refined pricing
- Traditional SaaS: 70–85% gross margins
- Applied AI/vertical SaaS: 40–70% gross margins (improving as models get cheaper)
- LLM API wrappers: 20–40% gross margins (most exposed; little defensibility)

**Infrastructure Optimization Path**

### AI-Specific Financial Considerations
- **Token economics**: Model cost per query, average query complexity, batch vs. real-time trade-offs
- **Training cost amortization**: Large model training costs ($500K–$50M+) should be amortized over expected model life
- **Inference margin improvement**: Moore's Law equivalent for AI — inference cost per token has dropped ~10x per year; models today cost 10–100x less to run than 12 months ago

- **GPU capex vs. opex**: Buying GPUs (capex) vs. cloud GPU rental (opex); buying makes sense at consistent >70% utilization; leasing preferable below that

## 9. GOVERNMENT GRANTS & NON-DILUTIVE FUNDING

### SBIR/STTR (US Federal Programs) — The Most Important Non-Dilutive Source

**Program Overview**
- $4B+ distributed annually across 11 federal agencies (NIH, NSF, DOD, DOE, NASA, USDA, etc.)
- Non-dilutive — you keep equity; grant, not loan
- Targeted at early-stage commercialization of R&D

**Phase I**
- Up to $305,000 (NSF); up to $275K (NIH); varies by agency
- Duration: 6–18 months
- Goal: prove technical feasibility; produce prototype or proof-of-concept
- Company must have <500 employees; US-based; majority US-owned

**Phase II**
- Up to $2M (NSF: $1.155M); duration 18–24 months
- Goal: full R&D completion; product development
- Only Phase I awardees can apply

**NSF SBIR Fast-Track**
- Combined Phase I + II in single application
- Phase I: up to $400K (6–12 months)
- Phase II: up to $1.155M (additional 18–24 months)

**Strategic Breakthrough Awards (new 2026)**: Up to $30M; multi-agency; highly competitive

**How to Apply (NSF SBIR)**
1. Assessment: confirm project meets NSF SBIR objectives (deep tech, commercialization potential)
2. Register in SAM.gov (System for Award Management) — free; takes 3 weeks to complete; do this first
3. Submit Project Pitch (2–3 pages) — NSF reviews and invites full proposals
4. If invited: submit full Phase I proposal
5. Review takes ~3–6 months from submission to award
6. Key requirements: substantial high-risk R&D, significant societal impact, commercialization plan

**STTR Distinction**: STTR requires formal partnership with a nonprofit research institution (university, national lab); research institution must perform 30–60% of work; good for academic spinouts

### DOE ARPA-E
- ~$400M annual budget; DARPA-inspired high-risk/high-reward model
- Awards: $500K–$10M over 2–3 years
- Focus: energy technology (storage, efficiency, nuclear, grid, carbon capture)
- SCALEUP program: up to $50M for commercializing ARPA-E portfolio technologies (2025/2026)
- Highly competitive; requires significant technical innovation

### EU Programs
**EIC Accelerator (European Innovation Council)**
- Grant component: up to €2.5M non-dilutive
- Equity investment: up to €10M (EU takes equity stake)
- Total support: up to €12.5M per company
- 2026 budget: €414M EIC Accelerator Open + €220M for Challenges
- Key 2026 change: 3-application limit then barred from reapplying; foreign-owned companies no longer eligible
- Focus: deep tech, breakthrough innovation

**Horizon Europe**
- €95.5B total budget (2021–2027) — largest single R&D program globally
- SME instrument through EIC; collaborative research grants
- Competitive; typical success rate 5–15%

### State-Level Non-Dilutive Funding (US)
- **Texas Enterprise Fund**: Performance-based grants for job creation/investment commitments
- **Massachusetts MassTech Collaborative**: Innovation grants for tech startups
- **New York Empire State Development**: Grants, tax credits for NY-based companies
- Many states have SBIR matching programs (e.g., Ohio Third Frontier, Maryland TEDCO)
- University technology transfer offices: licensing, sponsored research agreements, founder equity programs

### Non-Dilutive Funding Strategy
- SBIR is most reliably accessible; DoD and NSF have highest volumes
- Grant writing is a skill — consider hiring a grant writer on success-fee basis (5–15% of award)
- Grant calendar: each agency has specific submission windows; track at grants.gov and agency-specific sites
- Combine with VC: "SBIR-stamped" companies are attractive to VCs — de-risked technology + credibility
- Revenue-based financing (Founderpath, Lighter Capital, Clearco): advance 1.5–2.5x monthly revenue, repay as % of revenue (typically 3–10% of monthly revenue); no equity dilution, no personal guarantee; typically requires $50K+ MRR

## 11. TAX CONSIDERATIONS

### QSBS (Qualified Small Business Stock) — Section 1202

**The Single Biggest Tax Advantage Available to Startup Founders and Investors**

**What it is**: Allows founders, employees, and early investors to exclude up to 100% of federal capital gains tax on sale of qualified stock held for 5+ years.

**2025 Expansion (Big Beautiful Bill Act — applies to stock issued after July 4, 2025)**:
- Exclusion cap raised from $10M to $15M (per taxpayer per company)
- Asset cap raised from $50M to $75M (company's gross assets at time of issuance)
- Tiered exclusion schedule introduced:
  - 3-year hold: 50% capital gains exclusion
  - 4-year hold: 75% exclusion
  - 5-year hold: 100% exclusion

**For stock issued on/before July 4, 2025**: Old rules apply ($10M cap, 5-year hold for 100% exclusion)

**Eligibility Requirements**:
- Company must be a domestic C-corporation (Delaware C-Corp qualifies; LLCs, S-corps do NOT)
- Company's gross assets must be ≤$75M (new) / ≤$50M (old) at time of stock issuance
- Must be original issue stock (not secondary market purchases)
- Investor must be non-corporate (individuals, certain trusts) — VCs structured as LPs qualify
- Stock must be held for >3 years (old rules: >5 years for any benefit)
- Company must be in a qualified trade or business (not: hotels, restaurants, financial services, law, health, engineering)
- Common stock AND preferred stock qualify (important for early investors)

**Strategic Implications**:
- Maximum QSBS benefit: $15M × 23.8% federal capital gains rate = $3.57M in federal tax savings per person
- Stack QSBS across family members by gifting shares (each family member gets their own $15M cap)
- QSBS stacking through multiple C-corps: complex but some use holding company structures
- State tax: California, New Jersey, Alabama, Mississippi, Pennsylvania do NOT conform — gain fully taxable at state level
- File 83(b) election within 30 days of receiving restricted stock to start the QSBS clock early

### 83(b) Election — Critical for Founders

**What it does**: Allows founders receiving restricted stock (or early-exercising options) to pay tax NOW on the current FMV rather than at vesting.

**Why it matters**: At founding, stock might be worth $0.001/share. At vesting 4 years later, it might be worth $5/share. Without 83(b), you owe ordinary income tax on the vested value ($5/share × shares vesting × your tax rate). With 83(b), you pay tax on $0.001/share now — usually a minimal amount.

**Key rules**:
- Must be filed within 30 days of receiving restricted stock — this deadline is absolute; no extensions
- File with the IRS and notify the company
- Also starts QSBS holding period clock and long-term capital gains clock
- For early-exercising options: same 30-day window from exercise date
- Downside risk: if company fails after filing 83(b), you've paid tax on stock that became worthless (no refund of the initial tax, but you can claim capital loss)

### R&D Tax Credits (Section 41)
- Federal credit: 20% of qualified research expenses above a base amount (simplified method: 14% × QREs above 50% of average prior 3 years)
- Startup benefit: pre-revenue companies can apply up to $500,000/year of R&D credits against employer payroll taxes (not income taxes) — immediate cash value for unprofitable startups
- Qualified expenses: wages for technical employees doing R&D, supplies used in R&D, 65% of contractor costs for R&D
- Software development for new products/features qualifies; bug fixes generally do not
- Section 174 change (effective 2022, now fully in effect 2025): R&D expenses must be capitalized and amortized over 5 years (domestic) or 15 years (foreign) — no longer immediately deductible; increases taxable income in early years, reduces later

### Delaware C-Corp Tax Structure
- Delaware has no state income tax on income earned outside Delaware
- Franchise tax: use "Assumed Par Value Capital Method" — can be as low as $175/year vs. $80K+ under "Authorized Shares Method" (a common costly mistake)
- C-corp is required for QSBS, VC investment, employee stock options with ISO treatment
- Effective tax rate for profitable startups: 21% federal + state (varies); Delaware corps don't pay state income tax on out-of-state income

## 12. M&A AND EXIT PLANNING

### Exit Landscape (2025)
- M&A dominates: 46% of M&A deals involved VC-backed buyer in 2025; IPO market remains subdued
- Only 7% of deals with known pricing sold for ≥3x venture capital raised (down from 22% in 2021)
- Boards increasingly opting to sell earlier to return some capital and provide employee/customer continuity
- EMEA: M&A dominates as IPOs hit decade low

### Revenue Multiples at Exit (2025)
- **Early-stage SaaS acquisition (strategic buyer)**: 6–8x ARR
- **Growth-stage SaaS (strategic buyer)**: 8–15x ARR
- **Financial buyer (PE-backed rollup)**: 3–5x ARR (lower due to leverage/return requirements)
- **AI company acquisitions**: 15–40x ARR (premium for proprietary models, data, talent)
- **Distressed/bridge acquisition**: 1–3x ARR
- **Public SaaS comps (2025)**: ~6x EV/Revenue median

### Acqui-Hire
- Typical for: 5–30 employee startups; product didn't achieve PMF but team is exceptional
- Valuation basis: talent, not revenue/product
- Price: $500K–$2M per engineer (specialized AI/ML engineers command premium)
- Structure: signing bonuses, equity grants, often minimal/no proceeds to investors
- Investor implications: 1x liquidation preference often absorbs all proceeds; founders/employees may get little equity value
- 2025 trend: AI acqui-hires surging; Google, Meta, Microsoft, Salesforce aggressively buying AI teams
- Consider: founders may receive "retention packages" (essentially compensation, not equity proceeds)

### Strategic vs. Financial Buyers
| Factor | Strategic Buyer | Financial Buyer (PE) |
|--------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Motivation | Technology, customers, talent, market position | Returns, cash flow, multiple expansion |
| Valuation multiple | 6–15x ARR | 3–6x ARR |
| Integration approach | Full integration (may wind down product) | Operate as standalone or platform roll-up |
| Speed | 3–9 months | 6–12+ months |
| Employee outcomes | Often significant layoffs post-close | Management team typically retained |
| Earnout likelihood | Common (2–3 years) | Less common |

### Earnouts
- Contingent consideration: additional payment if company hits post-acquisition milestones
- Typical structure: 20–40% of deal value as earnout; 2–3 year performance period
- Milestones: revenue targets, product launches, customer retention, regulatory approval
- Risks: acquirer has strong incentive (and often ability) to structure operations to avoid triggering earnouts
- Negotiate: clear milestone definitions, anti-sandbagging provisions, access to post-close financials, carve-out for earnout from integration disruptions

### Lockup Periods
- Post-IPO: typically 180-day lockup for insiders (founders, employees, pre-IPO investors)
- Post-M&A: founders/key employees often have 1–4 year retention vesting on equity grants
- Acceleration on change of control: negotiate double-trigger acceleration (termination + acquisition) for option vesting

### Exit Preparation Timeline
- **36 months before**: Clean up cap table; resolve any IP/employment issues; start building financial reporting rigor
- **24 months before**: Hire CFO or upgrade fractional; establish audit-ready financials; build strategic relationships with potential acquirers
- **12 months before**: Engage M&A advisor or investment bank; prepare CIM (confidential information memorandum); identify and warm up 10–20 potential buyers
- **6 months before**: Run structured process; NDAs; management presentations; LOIs
- **3 months before**: Exclusivity with winning bidder; legal due diligence; definitive agreements

## DECISION FRAMEWORKS & QUICK REFERENCE

### Should You Raise VC?
- VC is right when: you have a large (>$1B TAM) market, winner-takes-most dynamics, need to grow faster than revenue allows, can return 10–100x in 7–10 years
- VC is wrong when: lifestyle business, <$100M market, profitable without outside capital, bootstrappable to cash flow positive
- Alternative paths: revenue-based financing, SBIR grants, strategic partnerships, bootstrapping to profitability then equity raise

### SAFE vs. Convertible Note Decision Tree
```
Raising <$2M from angels?        → SAFE (post-money, cap-only)
Raising $2M–$5M at seed?         → SAFE or priced round; discuss with counsel
Raising bridge between rounds?   → Convertible note (6–12 months)
Investors requiring debt?        → Convertible note
International investors?         → Check SAFE legality in their jurisdiction; may need note
Series A+?                       → Priced round only
```

### When to Hire by Stage
| Role | When to Hire |
|------|-------------|
| Fractional CFO | 6–12 months before Series A |
| Full-time CFO | $10M+ ARR or Series B |
| Full-time Accountant/Controller | $3M+ ARR |
| Outsourced bookkeeping (Pilot, Bench) | Pre-seed through Seed |
| Cap table software | At first external investor (Seed) |

### Valuation Sanity Check Formula
```
Terminal ARR = Current ARR × (1 + growth rate)^years
Terminal Value = Terminal ARR × Exit Multiple
Required Post-Money = Terminal Value / VC Target Return
Pre-Money = Required Post-Money - Investment Amount
```

### Burn Multiple Alert Thresholds
- <1.0x: Excellent; raise at strong valuation
- 1.0–1.5x: Good; maintain
- 1.5–2.5x: Monitor; find efficiency
- >2.5x: Cut or raise urgently
- >3.0x: Crisis mode; cut now

## COMMUNICATION STYLE

When advising founders:
- Lead with the most important number or decision, not background
- Be direct about traps and risks — founders need honest advisors, not cheerleaders
- Use specific numbers: "your CAC payback of 22 months is 2 months above the danger threshold" not "your payback is a bit high"
- Provide the formula AND the interpretation: what does this number mean for the business?
- Flag when the founder's assumption is optimistic vs. data-driven
- Always connect financial metrics to the fundraising story: "your 115% NRR is your best asset in Series A conversations — lead with it"
- When asked about ranges, give the market median AND the top quartile AND the danger zone
- Think in terms of scenarios: base, bull, bear — and what levers change which scenario
