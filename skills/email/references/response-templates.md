# Executive Response Templates

Templates for drafting replies in an executive's voice. These are starting points, not rigid scripts -- adapt tone, length, and content to match the specific email and the executive's style.

**General principles for executive email replies:**
- Be direct. Lead with the decision or answer, not the reasoning.
- Be brief. If it takes more than 5 sentences, consider whether a call would be faster.
- Be decisive. Hedging language ("maybe we could", "I was thinking perhaps") wastes everyone's time.
- Be human. One sentence of warmth or acknowledgment goes a long way.

---

## 1. Quick Acknowledgment

**When to use**: You've received something that needs review but you can't act on it right now. The sender needs to know you've seen it.

**Principle**: Acknowledge receipt AND set a time expectation. "Got it" alone leaves the sender wondering when they'll hear back.

**Template:**
```
Thanks, [Name]. Got it -- I'll review and get back to you by [specific timeframe].
```

**Variations:**
- With priority signal: `Thanks. Reviewing now -- will have an answer for you within the hour.`
- With delegation: `Got this. Pulling in [Person] to help me review -- we'll circle back by [time].`
- Minimal (for threads you're just tracking): `Tracking. Thanks for the update.`

---

## 2. Delegation

**When to use**: The request is legitimate but someone else should own it. Your job is to make the handoff clean.

**Principle**: Include enough context that the delegate can act without needing to ask you or the original sender a follow-up question. Name the person, explain why them, and set an expectation.

**Template:**
```
Looping in [Person] who owns [area/topic]. [Person], can you take point on this and keep [Sender] updated? [One sentence of context if the thread doesn't speak for itself.]
```

**Variations:**
- With deadline: `[Person], can you handle this by [date]? Let me know if you hit any blockers.`
- With guidance: `[Person] -- I'd suggest [approach]. Feel free to make the call on the details.`
- Downward delegation: `Assigning this to [Person] on my team. They'll be your point of contact going forward.`

---

## 3. Approval

**When to use**: Someone is waiting for your green light to proceed.

**Principle**: Approve clearly and remove ambiguity. If there are conditions, state them upfront. Don't make people guess whether "looks good" means "approved" or "interesting."

**Template:**
```
Approved. Go ahead. Let me know if you hit any blockers.
```

**Variations:**
- Conditional: `Approved with one change: [specific change]. Otherwise, go ahead.`
- Enthusiastic: `Love this. Approved -- let's move fast on it.`
- With visibility request: `Approved. Keep me posted on how the rollout goes.`

---

## 4. Decline / Redirect

**When to use**: You can't or shouldn't take this on, but you want to be helpful about it.

**Principle**: Be direct about the no, but offer an alternative path. Don't leave the person stuck -- point them somewhere useful.

**Template:**
```
Appreciate you thinking of me for this. [Person/Team] would be the right point of contact -- copying them here.
```

**Variations:**
- Timing-based: `Can't commit to this right now given [reason]. Happy to revisit in [timeframe] if it's still relevant.`
- Scope-based: `This falls outside what my team covers. [Person] in [Team] is who you want -- they handle [area].`
- Respectful pushback: `I don't think this is the right approach. Here's why: [brief reason]. Suggest we [alternative] instead.`

---

## 5. Information Request

**When to use**: You need more data before you can make a decision. Don't delay -- ask immediately.

**Principle**: Be specific about exactly what you need and by when. Vague requests ("can you send me more info?") create back-and-forth.

**Template:**
```
Before I can make a call on this, I need:
1. [Specific item]
2. [Specific item]
3. [Specific item]

Can you pull that together by [date]?
```

**Variations:**
- Quick question: `One question before I can approve: [question]?`
- Clarification: `Want to make sure I understand -- are you saying [interpretation A] or [interpretation B]?`
- Data request: `Can you share [specific metric/data] for [time period]? That'll help me evaluate this properly.`

---

## 6. Deferral

**When to use**: The topic is important but now isn't the right time to address it.

**Principle**: Acknowledge importance, name a specific future time, and make it clear you're not dismissing the request.

**Template:**
```
This is on my radar. Let's revisit at [specific time/meeting]. Can you send a reminder on [date]?
```

**Variations:**
- With reason: `Agree this is important. Right now we're focused on [priority]. Let's pick this up after [milestone].`
- With interim step: `Not ready to decide on this yet. Can you put together [analysis/proposal] and we'll discuss at [meeting]?`
- Batch it: `Adding this to the agenda for our [weekly/monthly meeting]. We'll address it then.`

---

## 7. Thread Closure

**When to use**: A discussion has reached a conclusion and you want to end the thread cleanly.

**Principle**: Summarize the decision, assign next steps, and close it. Long email threads that don't end clearly create confusion about what was actually decided.

**Template:**
```
Thanks everyone. Here's where we landed:
- [Decision or outcome]

Next steps:
- [Person]: [action] by [date]
- [Person]: [action] by [date]

Let's close this thread. Reach out directly if anything comes up.
```

**Variations:**
- Quick close: `Aligned. [Person], please capture this in [doc/ticket] and we're good.`
- Disagreement resolution: `I've heard both sides. Going with [decision] because [one-sentence reason]. [Person], please proceed.`
- Parking lot: `Good discussion but we're not ready to decide. Parking this for [timeframe]. [Person], please own bringing it back.`

## Tone Calibration

Match tone to the relationship and context:

| Relationship | Tone | Example opener |
|---|---|---|
| Board / investors | Formal, precise | "Thank you for flagging this." |
| CEO / executive peers | Direct, collegial | "Good call." / "Agree." |
| Direct reports | Warm, decisive | "Nice work." / "Let's do it." |
| External partners | Professional, clear | "Thanks for sending this over." |
| Vendors / cold outreach | Brief, neutral | (Often no response needed) |

When the executive's personal style is known, match it. Some executives are terse ("Approved."), others are warmer ("Love this, great work from the team."). Mirror whatever the executive naturally sounds like.
