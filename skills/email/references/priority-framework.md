# Email Priority Framework

An Eisenhower Matrix adapted for executive inbox triage. Use this framework to classify every email into exactly one tier. When in doubt between two tiers, choose the higher-urgency option -- it's cheaper to over-prioritize than to miss something critical.

---

## Tier 1: URGENT -- Respond Today

These emails carry time pressure or organizational risk. Missing them creates downstream problems.

**Signal patterns:**
- **Sender authority**: CEO, board members, investors, key clients, your direct manager
- **Deadline pressure**: Contains dates within 24 hours, words like "ASAP", "EOD", "by tomorrow", "time-sensitive", "blocker", "waiting on your approval"
- **Escalation language**: "Escalating", "flagging", "this has been outstanding", "second request", "following up again", "urgent"
- **Client-facing risk**: Customer complaints, deal-critical decisions, contract deadlines, SLA breaches
- **Crisis indicators**: Security incidents, PR issues, legal matters, system outages, employee emergencies
- **Meeting prep**: Materials for meetings happening today that require your input or decision

**Edge cases:**
- An email marked "urgent" by a vendor selling you something is NOT Tier 1 -- evaluate on content, not their label
- A recurring status report from an executive peer is usually Tier 2 or Tier 4, even if the sender is senior
- "Reply all" threads with escalation language: check if YOU specifically are being asked to act, or if you're just CC'd on someone else's fire

---

## Tier 2: IMPORTANT -- Respond This Week

These emails matter strategically but don't have immediate time pressure. Batching them is fine.

**Signal patterns:**
- **Direct reports needing decisions**: Approval requests, budget sign-offs, hiring decisions, strategy questions
- **Strategic topics**: Quarterly planning, org changes, product roadmap, partnership discussions
- **Cross-functional requests**: Peer executives asking for input, alignment meetings, joint initiatives
- **Upcoming meetings**: Agendas or pre-reads for meetings later this week
- **Performance and people**: 1:1 prep, feedback requests, team morale issues, retention concerns

**Edge cases:**
- A direct report asking for "quick feedback" on a doc -- if the doc is for a meeting today, this is Tier 1
- Board prep materials due next week -- Tier 2 now, but will become Tier 1 as the deadline approaches
- Recruiter emails for a critical open role -- Tier 2 if the pipeline is healthy, Tier 1 if you're about to lose the candidate

---

## Tier 3: DELEGATE -- Someone Else Should Handle This

The email needs action, but not from you specifically. Your value-add is routing it to the right person.

**Signal patterns:**
- **Operational requests**: Someone asking you about a process, tool, or detail that a team member owns
- **Expertise mismatch**: Technical questions better answered by an engineer, legal questions for counsel, financial details for finance
- **Information requests**: Data pulls, report generation, meeting scheduling, logistics
- **Vendor/partner communications**: Routine vendor management, procurement follow-ups, partnership operational details
- **Below your level of authority**: Tasks that don't require executive judgment -- approvals your delegates can handle

**When delegating, always:**
1. Identify the right person to handle it
2. Provide enough context that they can act without a follow-up question to you
3. Set an expectation for when you'd like an update (if relevant)

---

## Tier 4: DEFER -- Handle Later

Important enough to keep, but not important enough to handle now. Schedule it or batch it.

**Signal patterns:**
- **Long-form content**: Industry reports, thought leadership, conference invitations, training opportunities
- **Non-time-sensitive decisions**: Policy reviews, process improvements, tool evaluations
- **Requires deep thought**: Complex proposals, org design questions, strategic pivots that need reflection
- **Future planning**: Next quarter initiatives, annual review prep, budget planning (when the deadline is weeks away)
- **Nice-to-have meetings**: Optional attendance requests, networking invitations, informational sessions

**When deferring:**
- Flag the email for follow-up with a specific date if possible
- If it's been deferred twice already, it's probably Tier 5

---

## Tier 5: FYI / ARCHIVE -- No Response Needed

These emails are informational or noise. Read the subject line, skim if relevant, archive.

**Signal patterns:**
- **Automated notifications**: System alerts (that aren't outages), CI/CD reports, monitoring dashboards, subscription confirmations
- **Newsletters and digests**: Industry news, internal company newsletters, digest emails
- **CC-only threads**: You're copied for awareness but no one is asking you to act
- **Completed items**: "Done", "Resolved", "Closed" -- threads where the issue is already handled
- **All-hands announcements**: Company-wide communications you've already seen or that don't require action
- **Marketing and sales**: Cold outreach, vendor pitches, conference sponsorship requests

**Don't archive if:**
- The CC thread suddenly shifts to needing your input (re-evaluate as Tier 1 or 2)
- The system alert indicates an actual outage or security issue (Tier 1)
- The newsletter contains a regulatory change affecting your business (Tier 2)

---

## Priority Classification Process

When classifying an email:

1. **Check the sender first** -- Who sent it often determines urgency more than the subject line
2. **Scan for time words** -- Deadlines, dates, and urgency language are strong tier signals
3. **Assess who needs to act** -- If it's not you, it's Tier 3 regardless of urgency
4. **Default to action over inaction** -- When borderline, classify one tier higher and let the executive downgrade
