The Digital Threat Landscape

Before you can protect a brand, you need to see it through an attacker's eyes. What are they building, where, and how fast?

🤔 Before we continue — select all that apply

A customer receives a convincing email claiming their bank account is locked. They click a link and enter their password on what looks exactly like the bank's login page. Where could the attacker have hosted this fake page? Pick every option you think is plausible.

All four — and that's the problem. Attackers use every surface available. A single phishing campaign might serve the fake page from a typo-squatted domain, promote it via fake Google Ads, link it from a fake social media profile, and then route credentials to a Telegram channel on the dark web. Traditional perimeter security sees none of this. That's the gap Axur closes.

The six threat categories Axur monitors

🎣

📱

🏷️

💳

🌑

🕵️

Sponsored search results or display ads using your brand to divert traffic to malicious sites or competitors.

Why traditional security misses these threats

Traditional security thinking
"We have a firewall, endpoint protection, and a SIEM. Our perimeter is secured."
The gap: All these tools protect your infrastructure. None of them watch what attackers build outside your network.
What this actually means
An attacker can register yourbank-secure.com today, clone your login page, run a Google Ad pointing to it, and harvest credentials — without ever touching your network. Your firewall has nothing to inspect.

Digital risk protection is the discipline of monitoring the external attack surface: what exists about your brand on the open web, dark web, app stores, and social media that you don't control.
The Axur approach
Axur continuously crawls the open web, deep web, dark web, Telegram channels, app stores, social networks, and domain registrations. Anything matching your monitored assets — brand names, domains, BINs, executives — becomes an actionable ticket.

How fast does a phishing site get weaponized?

1
Domain registered (~$10, minutes)
Attacker buys a typo-squatted or brand-matching domain. Often dozens per campaign.
2
Fake page deployed (hours)
Clones your brand's visual identity using publicly available CSS and images. SSL certificates are free via Let's Encrypt — the padlock is no longer a trust signal.
3
Campaign launched (same day)
Phishing emails, social ads, or SMS blasts drive victims to the fake page. Credentials harvested in real-time to a Telegram bot.
4
Accounts drained (within hours)
Stolen credentials are tested immediately against banking and e-commerce accounts, or sold in underground markets.
Time is the critical variable
The sooner a phishing site is detected and taken down, the fewer victims it reaches. Axur's takedown service sends the first extrajudicial notification in under 4 minutes on average, and resolves 70% of cases on that first contact.

Anatomy of a ticket: what each field actually means

What it means

Key distinction: A site detected 30 days ago with a last detection from yesterday is still active and dangerous.
What it means
Detection date — when Axur first found this threat.

Last detection date — the most recent time Axur confirmed the threat is still live.

The gap between these tells you how long the threat has been active. A long-running phishing site that survived undetected has likely already victimized many users. Prioritize takedown and check whether credential reset communications are needed.

Working a Ticket: Taking Action

Six actions. Each moves a ticket forward differently. Here's when each one makes sense, and where teams typically go wrong.

The six actions: quick reference

⚠️
Create Incident
Flags ticket as a confirmed potential risk. Transitional — a further action must follow. This is not a resolution.
👁️
Send to Quarantine
Monitor URLs showing brand misuse but no active fraud. Axur checks daily at 6 a.m. for content changes. Your team manages quarantine, not Axur.
🛡️
Request Takedown
Axur sends extrajudicial notifications to ISP, registrant, and registrar. Cancellable within 3 minutes. Under 4 min to first notification on average.
🔧
Treat Internally
Your team handles removal without Axur involvement. Use the ticket to centralize progress. Axur takes no action.
🗑️
Discard
False positive or irrelevant. Closes the ticket. Use when the detection clearly does not constitute fraud.
✅
Add to Safelist
Mark a URL as authorized to circulate. All safelisted URLs are treated as safe in future detections. Use sparingly — mistakes are hard to catch later.

The takedown process — what happens after you click

1
Extrajudicial notification sent (under 4 min average)
Axur forwards a formal abuse notification to three entities simultaneously: the ISP (hosting the content), the registrant (who owns the domain), and the registrar (where the domain was purchased). Each has different leverage over the content.
2
First-contact resolution (70% of cases)
Seven in ten takedowns resolve on this first notification — the hosting provider or registrar disables the content. You'll see the ticket update in the platform as the URL goes dark.
3
Escalation if needed
For the 30% that don't resolve immediately, Axur escalates through additional channels. Overall resolution rate: 99%. Some delays occur when content is hosted in jurisdictions with limited cooperation.
4
Post-takedown monitoring
Attackers often re-register variants of taken-down domains or move to a new host. Axur continues monitoring for resurfaces. If the same content reappears, a new ticket is created automatically.
Takedown interrupted
Some takedowns enter an "Interrupted" status. This means Axur could not reach the responsible entity — common reasons include hosting in non-cooperative jurisdictions, invalid WHOIS contact data, or the content moving hosts after notification. You can request a Deep & Dark Web investigation to pursue further if your plan includes research hours.

Data Leakage: Protecting Your Crown Jewels

Card numbers, credentials, source code, internal documents: when they show up somewhere they shouldn't, every minute before you respond is time attackers have to use them.

Four leakage types, four different risk profiles

TypeWhat's exposedPrimary riskKey response action
Credit Card Exposure Card numbers, CVVs, expiry dates matched against your BINs Fraudulent transactions before you can block cards Reissue or block affected cards; notify cardholders
Credentials Exposure Login pairs (email + password) for employee or customer accounts Account takeover, lateral movement, customer fraud Force password reset; notify affected users; review MFA coverage
Code & Secret Leaks Source code, API keys, tokens, private certificates on GitHub or paste sites Credential exposure, IP theft, backdoor creation Revoke secrets immediately; audit for downstream use; DMCA if applicable
Mentions in Data Breach Files referencing your brand/domain inside ransomware leak packages Extortion, operational data exposure, third-party breach impact Download file in sandbox; assess scope; engage incident response if live attack
Critical: Takedown is not available for data breach files
Ransomware groups operate outside legal jurisdictions. Takedown isn't an option. The response is entirely internal: assess, contain, notify, harden. For exposure before monitoring was active, use Threat Hunting to search historical data.

Credit card exposure — what makes it different

Common misconception
"We have fraud detection — it will catch fraudulent transactions before they succeed."
The gap: Transaction-based fraud detection is reactive. Credit card exposure is proactive.
What exposure monitoring adds
Behavioral fraud detection catches anomalous transactions. Credit card exposure catches compromised cards before any transaction is attempted.

When Axur finds your card data in a Telegram channel or dark web market, you can reissue those cards before attackers use them. The Luhn algorithm validates every detected card number — invalid numbers are discarded, reducing false positives.

Complete cards (number + expiry + CVV) are the highest risk — they can be used immediately for CNP (card-not-present) fraud. Incomplete cards still warrant investigation but carry lower immediate risk.

Investigating a breach file — safe procedure

1
Read the detection details first
Before downloading anything, read the Source, Group, Context, and Mentioned Sample fields. Often these tell you enough to assess severity without touching the file.
2
Download in a sandbox environment
Files come as collected and have not been scanned for malware. Axur explicitly recommends using a sandbox. Never open breach files directly on your work machine or corporate network.
3
Assess scope: what exactly is in the file?
Determine: which systems or data types are referenced, whether it's a direct exfiltration or a third-party breach that mentions you, and the volume of records.
4
Escalate and notify internally
Notify owners of impacted systems. If the breach indicates an active attack, escalate immediately. Engage legal if regulatory notification (GDPR, PCI-DSS, etc.) may be required.
5
Update the detection status and document
Move to "In Treatment," add notes with your findings, and update to "Solved" when remediation is complete. Files are available for download for one year — 1 TB per customer per month limit.

Threat Hunting: Searching on Your Terms

Axur's monitoring runs 24/7 in the background and automatically opens tickets when matches surface against your assets. Threat Hunting is the complement: a user-initiated search where you formulate a hypothesis and query the data lake directly.

Automated monitoring vs. user-initiated Threat Hunting
Monitoring is automated and continuous — it runs 24/7 without your input and creates tickets when it finds matches against your configured assets. Threat Hunting is user-initiated and hypothesis-driven: you query the data lake manually when you want to (1) test a specific incident hypothesis, (2) explore historical data from before monitoring was active for an asset, (3) deepen context on an existing ticket, or (4) investigate an angle monitoring isn't configured to cover.

The Axur Query Language (AQL)

AQL is the search language for Threat Hunting. It's designed to be readable — if you've ever used a search engine's advanced operators, most of this will feel familiar.

OperatorSyntaxWhat it does
AND phishing AND mybank Both terms must appear in the result
OR mybank OR "my bank" Either term matches — useful for brand name variations
NOT mybank NOT official Exclude results containing a term — reduces noise
* wildcard mybrank* Matches zero or more characters — catches typo-squatting variants
? wildcard mybrank? Matches exactly one character — more precise than *
~ fuzzy mybank~ Fuzzy match — catches misspellings and character substitutions (e.g. mybonk, mYbank)

Real-world query examples

# Hunt for similar domain names — classic typosquat detection
domain:mybank* NOT mybank.com
# Find credential leaks for your domain
email:*@mybank.com AND type:credential
# Search for dark web mentions with executive names
("John Smith" OR "Jane Doe") AND mybank
# Find phishing kits referencing your brand in ads
title:mybank~ AND type:sponsored_ad
