# Behavioral Psychology Reference

## Why this stage exists

Most investment analysis is performed by people who already own the stock, which means most investment analysis is rationalizing a position rather than analyzing one. The behavioral bias audit is not optional for owners — it is the highest-leverage part of the entire process, because the analytical quality of Stages 1–7 means nothing if the user can't hold through a drawdown or recognize when their thesis has broken.

The goal of this stage is not to make the user feel bad. It is to surface biases that will cause expensive mistakes later.

---

## The five mandatory questions for owners

Ask these directly and in sequence. Wait for answers. Do not accept vague answers — push for specific, written responses.

---

### Question 1: The "zero-shares" test

> "If you had zero shares today — no position, no cost basis, no memory of what this stock has done — would you buy the exact same position size at the current price?"

**What this reveals**: Ownership bias (also called "endowment effect"). People overvalue what they already own. A "yes" to this question is required to justify holding. A "no" or "probably not" means the current position size is at least partly driven by ownership inertia, not conviction.

**Common hedged answers and what they mean**:
- "I'd probably buy a smaller position" → The user is over-positioned relative to their actual conviction. Consider trimming.
- "I'd want to do more research first" → The user hasn't actually completed their analysis. The position is ahead of their understanding.
- "Yes, definitely" → Check whether this is genuine conviction or defensive response. Follow up with Question 3.

**Special case — flat cost basis**: If the user is flat (bought at roughly current price), they are psychologically in a different state than if they're up 3x or down 40%. Flat is often the most dangerous state — they feel they've "avoided a loss" but haven't validated the thesis. Be especially probing here.

---

### Question 2: The written sell trigger

> "What specific, observable data point would make you sell this position? Give me one number and one event."

**Why written**: Unwritten sell triggers don't exist. Without a written trigger, "I'll sell when the thesis breaks" becomes infinite rationalization as the price falls and each piece of bad news gets explained away.

**What good looks like**:
- "If Q3 revenue misses guidance by more than 5%, I'll exit half."
- "If the SEC announces a consent decree requiring changes to the core product, I'll exit fully."
- "If operating margin drops below 40% for two consecutive quarters, I'll reassess."
- "If a major customer (>15% of revenue) announces they're leaving, I'll exit immediately."

**What bad looks like**:
- "If the business deteriorates." (Too vague — deteriorates how? By how much?)
- "If the stock drops below $X." (Price-based triggers are fine for position management but shouldn't be the primary thesis trigger)
- "If I see something that really concerns me." (This is not a trigger, it's a mood)

If the user can't fill in a specific trigger, this is the most important gap in the analysis. Walk them to one.

---

### Question 3: The steelmanned bear case

> "What is the strongest possible bear argument against this stock? State it in the bear's own words, as forcefully as you can. Then tell me specifically how you respond to it — with facts, not narrative."

**What this reveals**: Whether the user has genuinely engaged with the downside thesis or just built a one-sided bull narrative. People who can steelman the bear case and respond to it specifically have done real work. People who say "I've read the bear case and it's wrong" without specifics haven't.

**How to evaluate their answer**:
- Can they name the specific risk? (Not "competition" but "Meta could build competing SDK-free mobile ad targeting")
- Can they state why it's plausible? (Not "the shorts are wrong" but "here's why identifier bridging could be legal")
- Do they address it with specific evidence? (Not "management says it's fine" but "here's the technical rebuttal")

If they can't do this, they haven't analyzed the stock — they've built a narrative. Gently push them to either do the work or acknowledge the thesis has a hole.

---

### Question 4: Facts vs. projections vs. hopes

> "Look at the key elements of your bull thesis. For each one, tell me: is this a fact (observable today), a projection (reasonably expected given current trends), or a hope (would be nice but there's no specific evidence for it)?"

**Why this matters**: Valuation models and conviction levels should be calibrated to what tier the supporting evidence lives in. If the entire bull case is built on hopes dressed up as projections, the position deserves low conviction and small sizing.

**Sample framework for the user to apply**:

| Claim | Tier | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Company is growing at X% | Fact | Observable in last quarter's results |
| Growth will continue at Y% for 3 years | Projection | Based on product pipeline, market size, and backlog |
| E-commerce expansion will add $1.5B in revenue | Hope | No customer concentration data, early friction reports |
| Margins will stay above 80% | Hope | Assumes no regulatory or competitive pressure |

**The investment implication**: The more hopes are doing the work in the thesis, the lower the maximum position size should be. Hope-heavy theses deserve exploratory sizing (0.5–2%), not conviction sizing (5–10%).

---

### Question 5: Drawdown durability

> "If this stock dropped 40% from your cost basis — not from the peak, from what you paid — what would you do? Would you add, hold, or sell? What would your emotional state be? Would you lose sleep?"

**What this reveals**: The user's actual risk tolerance, not their stated risk tolerance. These are very different.

**Important distinctions to probe**:
- "I'd add at -40%" → Fine if they have a thesis and written trigger. But have they actually experienced a 40% drawdown in a single name? The first time is very different from what they imagine.
- "I'd probably hold but it would be stressful" → This is honest. The question then becomes: can the position size be adjusted so the stress is manageable?
- "I'm not sure I could handle that" → The current position is already too large, regardless of the analysis quality.

**The dollar test**: Don't talk in percentages. Talk in dollars. "If this position is 3% of your portfolio and you're down 40%, that's [X]% of your total portfolio. Can you sit with that number on a screen for 6 months while the thesis plays out?" Percentages are abstract. Dollar losses are visceral. Match the position size to the actual human capacity.

## Specific biases to check for

Beyond the five questions, watch for these in the conversation:

**Anchoring to peak price**: "It's down 40% from its high" is not the same as "it's cheap." The ATH was set before material negative information arrived. The ATH is irrelevant to current fair value.

**Recency bias on growth**: "It's been growing 70% for 3 years" is not evidence it will grow 70% for 3 more years. Regression to the mean is powerful. High growth rates attract competition.

**Narrative over data**: When the user says "I just believe in this company" or "the CEO is brilliant" as the primary thesis support, the narrative is doing the work that analysis should be doing.

**Loss-recovery bias**: "I need to hold to get back to even" is not a thesis. The current price and the cost basis are completely independent questions. The market doesn't know or care what you paid.

**Social proof from performance**: "Everyone is buying this" or "it's been the best performer this year" tells you about past returns, not future ones. Often the signal is reversed — high recent performance attracts excess capital that drives future underperformance.

**Confirmation bias in research**: "I've read 10 bullish articles about this stock" is not research. Research requires deliberately seeking the opposing view.

---

## When to escalate the bias concern

In certain situations, the bias audit reveals a situation serious enough to pause the full analysis and address it directly:

1. **User just bought a large position without understanding the business** → Stop, say this clearly, do not proceed to sizing without first establishing thesis clarity.
2. **User is holding a large position in a stock they'd never buy today** → Acknowledge the endowment effect and help them think about whether to trim.
3. **User wants to add to a losing position without articulating what changed** → This is often loss-recovery bias. Challenge it directly.
4. **User can't survive the bear case dollar loss** → Position is already too large. The analysis is done; the immediate action is to reduce.

These situations require directness, not analysis. Delivering a rigorous 8-stage analysis to someone who is about to make a behaviorally-driven mistake is not helpful — it adds intellectual cover to a bad decision.

## Bias audit score note

The **Thesis Clarity** score in Stage 8 is a direct output of this stage. It measures not the quality of the business, but the quality of the user's understanding of the business and their own process.

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 9–10 | Can articulate thesis, bear case, sell triggers, and position fit from first principles. |
| 7–8 | Solid understanding with minor gaps. Sell triggers partially formed. |
| 5–6 | General understanding but relying on external sources. Key risks acknowledged but not fully resolved. |
| 3–4 | Limited understanding. Thesis is largely borrowed narrative. Sell triggers vague. |
| 1–2 | Owns stock without knowing why. Analysis is rationalization. Highly vulnerable to emotional selling. |

*A Thesis Clarity score below 5 should result in a "do not add" recommendation regardless of the business quality scores.*
