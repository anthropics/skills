# Financial Analysis Reference

## Core principle

Report the numbers, but then ask what they're telling you that the headline numbers don't say. A company with $30B revenue and negative FCF is worse off than a company with $2B revenue and +$500M FCF. The financial analysis is not accounting — it's interrogation.

---

## The full checklist

### Revenue and growth quality

- **YoY revenue growth rate** — and trend (accelerating, stable, decelerating?)
- **Organic vs. inorganic growth** — was growth driven by acquisitions? Organic growth is more durable
- **Revenue concentration** — what % comes from the top 1, 5, 10 customers? >20% from a single customer is significant risk
- **Revenue type** — subscription/recurring vs. transactional vs. hardware? Recurring revenue is worth more (lower churn, more predictable)
- **Geographic concentration** — what % from the top 1–2 markets? Regulatory risk concentrates here
- **RPO / backlog growth vs. revenue growth** — bookings ahead of revenue is a leading indicator; bookings behind revenue means future deceleration

**Red flag**: Revenue growing fast but deceleration rate is faster than consensus expected. The market doesn't punish deceleration — it punishes surprise deceleration.

---

### Margin structure and trajectory

Track all three over 3+ years, not just the latest quarter:

| Metric | What it measures |
|---|---|
| Gross margin | Pricing power and cost structure of the core product |
| Operating margin | Operating leverage — does scaling the business improve profitability? |
| Net margin | All-in profitability including interest and taxes |
| EBITDA margin | Cash-generating ability before capex (watch for abuse as an "adjusted" number) |
| FCF margin | Real cash arriving after everything, including capex |

**The key tension**: Expanding gross margins with compressing FCF margins often means SBC is quietly eating the profits. Always check.

**Margin sustainability rule**: Very high margins (>75% gross, >50% EBITDA) attract competition and regulatory scrutiny. Never assume current peak margins are the through-cycle number. Run sensitivity analysis: what does the valuation look like if margins compress 15–20 percentage points over 3 years?

---

### Free cash flow — the real number

FCF = Operating cash flow − Capex

Reported revenue and even net income can be managed. FCF is much harder to manufacture.

- **FCF vs. net income**: If FCF is consistently well below net income, ask why. Usually SBC, working capital buildup, or capex that isn't hitting the P&L. None of these are neutral.
- **FCF margin trend**: Is it expanding with scale? (Good — operating leverage.) Compressing? (Concerning — growth is expensive.)
- **FCF consistency**: Is it lumpy or smooth? Lumpy FCF often means the business is sensitive to deal timing or collections.
- **Capex intensity**: Low capex = high FCF multiplier. High capex = check whether it's maintenance (defending the business) or growth (investing for future revenue). They have very different implications.

### Balance sheet

- **Cash + short-term investments** — how much liquidity exists?
- **Total debt** — short-term (due within 12 months) separately from long-term
- **Net debt** (Total debt − Cash) — or net cash if cash exceeds debt
- **Net debt / EBITDA** — leverage ratio. <1x is conservative, 1–3x is moderate, >4x warrants scrutiny
- **Interest coverage ratio** (EBITDA / Interest expense) — can the company service its debt from operations? <3x is concerning, >10x is comfortable
- **Upcoming debt maturities** — are there large maturities in the next 2–3 years? Refinancing risk is real in high-rate environments
- **Covenant risk** — do credit agreements have maintenance covenants that could be triggered in a downside scenario?

**Deleveraging as a signal**: A company rapidly reducing net debt / EBITDA is demonstrating that its cash generation is real and management is disciplined. The reverse is also true.

---

### Returns on capital

These tell you whether the business is actually creating value or just growing:

- **Return on Equity (ROE)**: Net income / Shareholders' equity. >15% is good; >30% exceptional. Be careful with high ROE from high leverage — it's not the same thing.
- **Return on Invested Capital (ROIC)**: NOPAT / Invested capital. The gold standard. Businesses with ROIC consistently above their WACC are compounders. ROIC below WACC are value destroyers regardless of growth rate.
- **Return on Assets (ROA)**: Less distorted by capital structure than ROE.

---

### Stock-based compensation — the hidden dilution

SBC is a real cost. A company that reports "non-GAAP EPS" while excluding SBC is presenting a misleading picture of profitability.

Check:
- **SBC as % of revenue** — is it growing or stable? >15% of revenue is aggressive
- **Net dilution** (share count growth after buybacks) — is the company actually returning value to shareholders net of SBC, or is it diluting?
- **Buyback quality** — buying back shares at reasonable valuations is value creation; buying at peak multiples to offset SBC is capital destruction

*True test of management's capital discipline: Is the actual share count flat or declining?*

---

### Stress scenarios — always run at least one

**Bear scenario**: What does the stock look like if:
- Revenue growth decelerates from X% to Y% (pick the slower case)
- Margins compress 15–20 percentage points over 3 years
- The multiple re-rates to sector average or historical mean

**Bull scenario**: What does the stock look like if:
- The growth thesis plays out fully
- Margins hold or expand modestly
- The multiple stays at current level

**Base case**: Somewhere between. What do you think is actually most likely?

The question to answer from the stress test: "If the bear scenario happens, is it survivable (I'm down 30%, recover in 2 years) or catastrophic (I'm down 60%, the thesis is broken)?" If the bear scenario is catastrophic, position size must reflect that.

---

### Guidance quality — reading management credibility

Analyze the last 4–8 quarters of guidance vs. actuals:

- Does management consistently beat their own guidance? (Good — they're sandbagging appropriately)
- Do they consistently miss? (Bad — either sandbagging is impossible or execution is weak)
- Has guidance been cut mid-cycle? (Watch carefully — the language used during cuts often foreshadows more cuts)
- Did they beat the last quarter but guide below consensus? (Often signals visible softness they're not directly communicating)

**The most important earnings call event**: A strong beat that triggers a stock decline. The market is telling you it's pricing in something the headline numbers don't show. Investigate what that is.

### Summary financial health score

Use this for the **Financial Strength** dimension in Stage 8 scoring:

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 9–10 | Fortress balance sheet. Net cash. FCF growing faster than revenue. ROIC well above WACC. |
| 7–8 | Strong cash generation. Manageable leverage. Margins expanding or stable. |
| 5–6 | Solid but with one or two meaningful concerns (high leverage, margin compression, SBC burden). |
| 3–4 | Financially stressed. Negative FCF, high leverage, or structural margin problem. |
| 1–2 | Requires external capital to survive. Existential financial risk. |
