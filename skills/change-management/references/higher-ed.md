# Higher Education Change Management Reference

## Governance & Authority Structures
- **Shared governance**: Faculty senate holds co-authority over curriculum, academic policies, faculty affairs — changes in these domains *require* faculty senate involvement, not just consultation
- **Provost / Academic Affairs**: Academic program changes flow through this office
- **Board of Trustees/Regents**: Approves major structural changes, degree programs, budget reallocation
- **Accreditors**: SACSCOC, HLC, MSCHE, WSCUC — structural changes may trigger substantive change reports (advance notice of 6–12 months required)
- **Student Government**: Advisory authority; high symbolic importance for student-facing changes

## Shared Governance Navigation
- **Do not mistake consultation for approval** — faculty must have genuine input that can alter outcomes
- Allow adequate deliberation time — faculty senate cycles typically meet monthly; plan 2–4 cycles for significant proposals
- Build a **faculty change champion network** before going through formal governance channels
- Distinguish between: Academic policy (requires faculty senate) vs. Administrative operations (does not)

## Change Pace Constraints
- Academic calendar governs everything — changes affecting curriculum require catalog deadlines (typically 12–18 months ahead)
- Budget cycles: fiscal year planning windows (typically Oct–Feb for following AY) must be respected
- Faculty hiring: 9–12 month lead time minimum for tenure-track positions
- Accreditation substantive change: 6–12 months advance notification; approval before implementation

## Effective Engagement Channels
| Audience | High-Trust Channels | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Faculty | Faculty senate committees, department chairs, collegial conversations | Email fatigue is high; in-person or small-group works better |
| Department Chairs | Chairs council, direct engagement with Dean | Key middle-layer; often underpowered in change processes |
| Staff | Staff council, HR communications, manager cascade | Often overlooked in academic change processes |
| Students | Student government, town halls, course-level communication | Short institutional memory (4-year cohort turnover) |
| Deans | Cabinet-level briefings, 1:1 with Provost | Gate-keepers for college-level implementation |

## Common Resistance Patterns
- **Academic freedom concerns**: Any change perceived as constraining faculty autonomy or curriculum decisions
  - *Mitigation*: Clearly delineate what is and is not within faculty purview; involve faculty in design
- **"Not invented here" syndrome**: Resistance to external mandates (state system, accreditor, industry trends)
  - *Mitigation*: Reframe as internal adaptation; let faculty take ownership of the solution
- **Resource competition**: Departments protecting FTE, budget lines, or course enrollments
  - *Mitigation*: Transparent resource allocation process; cross-departmental benefit framing
- **Administrative skepticism**: Faculty distrust of administrative motives — especially post-austerity or layoffs
  - *Mitigation*: Data transparency; shared governance fidelity; acknowledge institutional history

## Alignment Touchpoints
- Institutional strategic plan
- Middle States / regional accreditation self-study
- Program review cycles
- IPEDS reporting obligations
- HEA compliance (Title IV, Title IX, ADA)
- State authorization requirements (for online programs)
