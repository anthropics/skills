# Nonprofit Change Management Reference

## Governance & Authority Structures
- **Board of Directors**: Fiduciary authority; approves major strategic shifts, budget realignments, executive transitions
- **Executive Director**: Operational authority; primary change sponsor for internal initiatives
- **Funder relationships**: Major funders may have contractual expectations tied to program delivery — changes affecting grant-funded programs require funder notification and sometimes approval
- **Advisory boards**: Influential but non-binding; valuable for legitimacy, not decision authority

## Unique Nonprofit Change Dynamics
- **Mission drift risk**: Any change must be explicitly connected to mission — staff and boards are highly attuned to this
- **Resource scarcity**: Change initiatives compete with direct service delivery for limited staff capacity
- **Volunteer/staff duality**: Some roles blur paid/unpaid; engagement strategies must account for both
- **High turnover**: Nonprofits average 19–20% annual turnover — build change into onboarding, not just current staff
- **Founder's syndrome**: In founder-led orgs, change may be resisted as a threat to the founder's identity/vision — Bridges' Transition Model is critical here

## DEI-Centered Change Considerations
- Assess who benefits from and who is burdened by the change — disaggregate by race, gender, role level
- Ensure co-design processes include frontline staff and community members, not just leadership
- Name and address historical harms or inequities the change is designed to correct
- Use equity impact statements before finalizing implementation plans

## Effective Engagement Channels
| Audience | High-Trust Channels | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Program Staff | Team meetings, 1:1s with direct manager | High relationship-dependence; trust = manager trust |
| Board Members | Executive committee briefings, board retreats | Time-limited; need concise, strategic framing |
| Funders | Site visits, quarterly reports, narrative updates | Frame change as learning and improvement |
| Community/Clients | Focus groups, advisory councils, co-design sessions | Critical for legitimacy; often overlooked |
| Volunteers | Orientation updates, newsletter, volunteer coordinator | Often last to know; treat as full stakeholders |

## Common Resistance Patterns
- **"Mission purity" resistance**: Fear that structural/operational changes compromise values
  - *Mitigation*: Explicitly connect every change decision to mission statement language
- **Capacity resistance**: "We're already stretched" — real concern, not an excuse
  - *Mitigation*: Phase the change; protect program delivery bandwidth; add temporary support
- **Funder-driven change fatigue**: Staff resent pivots driven by grant requirements vs. community need
  - *Mitigation*: Be transparent about funder context; involve staff in shaping the response

## Funder Communication Strategy
- Proactive disclosure > reactive disclosure — notify before changes go public
- Frame change as: evidence-based learning / strategic adaptation / theory of change refinement
- Include funder in stakeholder map as "Keep Satisfied" or "Manage Closely" depending on grant size
- Update logic model and evaluation plan to reflect change

## Alignment Touchpoints
- Strategic plan / theory of change
- Grant deliverables and logic models
- Board-approved annual operating plan
- DEI strategic plan (if separate)
- Program evaluation framework
