# Employment Law for Tech Companies Reference

## Worker Classification (W-2 vs. 1099)

### Why It Matters
Misclassification exposes companies to: unpaid payroll taxes (employer's share of FICA, FUTA), unpaid overtime/minimum wage, missed benefits (health insurance, 401k, stock options), state penalties, and private lawsuits.

### Federal Standards

**IRS Common Law Test** (Revenue Ruling 87-41) — 20 factors grouped into three categories:

**Behavioral Control**:
1. Instructions given about how to do the work
2. Type of instructions (when, where, how)
3. Training provided to perform in a particular way

**Financial Control**:
4. Significant investment in tools/facilities
5. Services available to general public
6. Not reimbursed for business expenses
7. Opportunity for profit or loss
8. More permanent relationship expected

**Type of Relationship**:
9. Written contract
10. Employee benefits provided
11. Permanency of relationship
12. Whether services are key to company's core business

No single factor is determinative. IRS uses overall picture.

**DOL Economic Reality Test** (FLSA purposes, 6-factor test):
1. Extent to which work is integral to employer's business
2. Worker's opportunity for profit or loss
3. Extent of relative investment
4. Whether work requires special skills and initiative
5. Permanency of the relationship
6. Degree of control by employer

### California's ABC Test (AB5, Labor Code §2750.3)

Effective January 1, 2020. Presumption of employment status. To prove independent contractor status, hiring entity must establish **ALL THREE**:

**A**: Worker is free from control and direction in connection with performance of work (both under contract and in fact)
**B**: Worker performs work that is outside the usual course of the hiring entity's business
**C**: Worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, or business of the same nature as that involved in the work performed

**Prong B** is the killer for tech companies — a software engineer writing code for a software company fails this prong and is an employee.

**AB5 Exemptions** (many professions exempted): licensed professionals (doctors, lawyers, architects, accountants, insurance brokers), real estate licensees, hairstylists, certain tradespeople, entertainment industry. Gig drivers: exempted by Proposition 22 (2020) under specific conditions.

**Penalties for Misclassification in California:**
- Labor Code §226.8: $5,000–$25,000 per willful violation
- PAGA (Private Attorneys General Act): employees can bring representative actions; $100/employee/pay period (initial violation) + $200/employee/pay period (subsequent) → can reach $50,000–$200,000+ per misclassified worker
- EDD assessment: 15% penalty on unpaid taxes if negligent or intentional
- Back wages, overtime, meal/rest period premiums, business expense reimbursements

## Non-Compete Agreements

### State-by-State Enforceability

**Void/Unenforceable:**
- **California** (Bus. & Prof. Code §16600): all non-competes void regardless of where signed; even non-California employees working for California companies may be protected; applies to 1099 contractors too
- **Minnesota** (effective Jan 1, 2023): non-competes in employment contracts void
- **North Dakota**: non-competes void with narrow exceptions
- **Oklahoma**: non-competes void except for sale-of-business context

**Enforced with Reasonableness Test (majority of states):**
Non-compete must be: (1) ancillary to otherwise enforceable agreement, (2) reasonably limited in duration (6 months–2 years typical; 3+ years rarely upheld), (3) reasonably limited in geography or scope, (4) protect legitimate business interest (trade secrets, customer relationships, specialized training).

**Strong Enforcement (fewer restrictions):**
Florida, Texas (with specific requirements), Georgia, Virginia (limited by 2020 reform)

**FTC Non-Compete Rule (2024):**
FTC issued rule banning non-competes in May 2024. Federal court (N.D. Texas) enjoined enforcement in August 2024. Current status: enjoined, not enforceable. FTC likely to appeal or rulemaking process to continue. Monitor for developments. Even if ultimately void, demonstrates political trend against non-competes.

**Practical Note**: Non-solicitation of employees and customers are generally enforceable in most states including California (for employees) — these are legally distinct from non-competes.

## H-1B Specialty Occupation Visa

**Purpose**: Temporary work authorization for specialty occupation requiring at least bachelor's degree (or equivalent) in specific specialty.

**Cap**: 65,000 regular cap + 20,000 for US master's degree holders = 85,000/yr. Significantly oversubscribed (typically 400,000+ registrations for 85,000 spots).

**Lottery System (2025 FY2026 changes)**:
- Wage-based weighted lottery: registrations weighted by wage level
  - Level IV (top wage): 4 entries
  - Level III: 3 entries
  - Level II: 2 entries
  - Level I (entry): 1 entry
- Registration window: March (approximately) for October 1 start
- Registration fee: $215 per beneficiary

**Filing Fees (approximate 2025):**
- Form I-129 base fee: $780 (medium/large employers) or $460 (small employers)
- ACWIA training fee: $1,500 (26+ employees) or $750 (1–25 employees)
- Fraud prevention and detection fee: $500
- Premium Processing: $2,805 (15 business days)
- **Presidential proclamation (September 2025)**: $100,000 payment for cap-subject H-1Bs filed on behalf of beneficiaries outside US — highly controversial, litigation anticipated

**Duration**: 3 years initially, extendable to 6 years; unlimited extensions during green card process (pending I-140 >365 days).

**Cap-Exempt Employers**: universities, nonprofits affiliated with higher education, government research organizations — can hire H-1B workers at any time without going through lottery.

## O-1 Visa (Extraordinary Ability)

**O-1A**: Sciences, education, business, athletics — sustained national/international acclaim
**O-1B**: Arts, motion picture/TV — extraordinary achievement

**No annual cap, no lottery — file at any time.** Can be renewed indefinitely in 1-year increments. Can work for multiple employers (concurrent O-1s).

**Requirements**: meet at least 3 of 8 criteria (O-1A sciences/business):
1. Receipt of nationally/internationally recognized prizes or awards
2. Membership in associations requiring outstanding achievement
3. Published material about the person in major media
4. Judging the work of others
5. Original scientific, scholarly, artistic, athletic, or business contributions of major significance
6. Authorship of scholarly articles in professional journals or major media
7. Employment in critical/essential capacity for distinguished organization
8. High salary relative to others in the field

**Processing**: Standard 2–4 months; Premium Processing 15 business days ($2,805).
**Attorney fees**: ~$3,000–$7,000; government fees ~$500–$800.

## Permanent Residence (Green Card) Pathways

### EB-1A (Extraordinary Ability — Self-Petition)
No employer sponsorship required. No PERM labor certification. Self-file I-140.
Requirements: sustained national/international acclaim; must meet at least 3 of 10 criteria.
Processing: 6–19 months (2026 average); Premium Processing: 15 business days ($2,965).
Fees: $1,015–$1,315 government + $5,000–$12,000+ attorney.

### EB-1B (Outstanding Professor/Researcher)
Requires employer sponsorship. International recognition in academic field. Job offer at university or qualifying research institution. 3 years research experience.

### EB-2 NIW (National Interest Waiver — Self-Petition)
Waives PERM requirement and employer sponsorship. Must demonstrate: substantial merit and national importance of work; well positioned to advance endeavor; waiver of PERM in national interest.
Processing: 13–18 months average (2025); Premium Processing 45 business days.

### PERM-based EB-2/EB-3
Employer sponsors; Labor Certification (PERM) required to prove no qualified US workers available; PERM process: 6–12+ months; subject to per-country quotas (India, China have multi-decade backlogs for EB-2/EB-3).

## WARN Act

**Federal WARN Act** (29 U.S.C. §2101 et seq.):
- Applies to employers with 100+ employees
- 60 days advance written notice required for:
  - Plant closing affecting 50+ employees at single site within 30 days
  - Mass layoff of 500+ employees at single site within 30-day period, OR
  - Mass layoff of 50–499 employees constituting 33%+ of workforce at single site
- Notice to: affected workers (or union), state agency, local government chief elected official
- Penalties: back pay + benefits up to 60 days; civil penalty $500/day for violations

**California WARN Act** (more stringent):
- Applies to employers with 75+ employees
- 60 days notice for layoffs of 50+ employees within 30-day window
- Also applies to relocations of 100+ miles
- Includes temporary layoffs > 6 months
- Cal-WARN 2026 amendments: add requirements for notice content

**State Mini-WARN Acts** (18 states): CA, CT, HI, IL, IA, KS, ME, MA, MI, MN, NH, NJ, NY, OR, RI, SC, TN, WI — many lower employee thresholds or extend notice periods.

## Anti-Discrimination and Harassment

**Federal Protected Classes** (Title VII, ADEA, ADA, GINA, etc.): race, color, religion, sex, national origin, age (40+), disability, genetic information, pregnancy.

**Tech-Specific Concerns:**
- AI hiring tools with disparate impact on protected classes = discrimination even without intent
- Algorithmic management creating harassment conditions
- Remote work ADA accommodation obligations
- Pay equity (many states require pay transparency in job postings)

**EEOC Guidance on AI**: employers liable for discriminatory outcomes of AI systems they use, even if vendor created the system.

**Employee Handbooks**: document anti-harassment policy, complaint procedure, investigation process; mandatory training in California (5+ employees), New York, and other states.

**EPLI (Employment Practices Liability Insurance)**: covers wrongful termination, discrimination, harassment, retaliation claims. Cost: $1,000–$10,000/yr for startups depending on size and history.
