## 1. Product-Market Fit Frameworks

### The Sean Ellis Test (40% Rule)
Ask: *"How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?"* — Very Disappointed / Somewhat Disappointed / Not Disappointed / N/A. The 40% benchmark for "Very Disappointed" signals meaningful PMF.

**Critical caveats:**
- 40% is a signal, not a binary gate. Sample sizes below 100 are statistically unreliable (margin of error ±13% at n=50)
- B2B SaaS with complex multi-stakeholder buying may need different benchmarks
- **Most powerful use**: Isolate the "most disappointed" sub-segment — your real ICP often scores 60–70%+ when separated from the full respondent pool
- Always complement with retention curves (D7, D30, D90 cohorts) and qualitative JTBD interviews

### Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD)
People don't buy a quarter-inch drill — they buy a quarter-inch hole. JTBD reframes the unit of analysis from customer demographics to *the progress the customer is trying to make*.

**Timeline interview structure**: Walk the customer through the exact moment they decided to switch — what triggered the search, what alternatives they considered, what the "first thought" moment was.

**Job Statement format**: "When I [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]."

**Applied to product design**: Each feature should map to a job. JTBD + Kano model together drive prioritization that tracks to actual customer outcomes, not internal opinion.

### Wardley Mapping
Plot every component of your value chain on two axes: **visibility to user** (y) and **evolutionary stage** (x: Genesis → Custom → Product → Commodity).

**Strategic power**: See where you're competing on commoditized components (cost center, not moat) vs. Genesis/Custom stages (differentiation opportunity). As a component evolves toward Product, move innovation higher up the value chain. Maps also reveal **inertia** — where incumbents are stuck defending legacy positions, creating attack vectors for new entrants.

**Startup application**: Map competitor value chains alongside your own. Identify components they have as expensive Custom-stage builds you can purchase as commodities — giving you cost and speed advantages.

### Blue Ocean Strategy (ERRC Grid)
Four disciplined questions about every industry competitive factor:
- **Eliminate:** What does the industry take for granted that should be removed?
- **Reduce:** What factors should be reduced well below industry standard?
- **Raise:** What factors should be raised well above industry standard?
- **Create:** What factors should be created that the industry has never offered?

This breaks the value-cost trade-off by de-investing in low-value factors while creating new value sources. Airbnb eliminated hotel infrastructure costs while creating local authenticity.

### Crossing the Chasm
The critical gap between **early adopters** (visionaries who tolerate rough edges) and the **early majority** (pragmatists who want proven whole-product solutions). This chasm kills most startups that mistake early adopter enthusiasm for market validation.

**Beachhead strategy**: Select a single niche small enough to dominate completely before expanding. Criteria: (1) segment has compelling reason to buy, (2) reachable through a single channel, (3) a reference customer in this niche opens the next niche.

**Whole product concept**: Pragmatists need complete solutions — integrations, support, documentation, ecosystem. Startups that fail the chasm crossing offer features, not whole products.

## 2. Go-to-Market Strategy

### PLG vs. SLG vs. MLG Decision Framework

**Product-Led Growth (PLG):** Product is primary vehicle for acquisition, activation, expansion. Signals it's right: time-to-value under 15 minutes, individual users adopt without org buy-in, deal sizes under $5K/year.

**Sales-Led Growth (SLG):** Sales reps generate and close leads. Right when: product requires configuration to deliver value, multiple stakeholders must align, deal sizes exceed $25K/year.

**Marketing-Led Growth (MLG):** Demand generation drives awareness and leads. Most relevant when buyer research precedes vendor shortlisting, category education required.

**Critical finding**: Pure PLG vs. SLG is a false binary. The dominant 2025–2026 motion is **PLG-led, SLG-expanded** (hybrid): self-serve for SMB bottom-up land, sales for enterprise expansion. OpenView 2024 data: 67% of hybrid companies hit NRR targets vs. 58% of pure-PLG.

**PQL (Product Qualified Lead):** Free/trial user who has hit the activation threshold. Route PQLs to sales at the right moment — not too early (value not felt), not too late (already decided to stay free). Slack: messages sent. Dropbox: files stored. Figma: collaborators invited.

### ICP Development
ICP is not a persona — it's a set of firmographic and behavioral criteria that define the company type most likely to buy quickly, expand, and retain. An operational filter, not a marketing artifact.

**Development process:**
1. Analyze top 20% of customers by revenue + retention + expansion — what do they share?
2. Identify the "hair on fire" problem indicator — what triggers the buying decision?
3. Define negative ICP (who wastes your sales resources)
4. Score prospects on ICP fit before outreach
5. Closed-loop feedback from sales losses to refine criteria quarterly

### TAM/SAM/SOM
- **TAM**: Revenue opportunity at 100% market share — used for investor narrative
- **SAM**: Segment you can reach with your GTM model and product
- **SOM**: What you can realistically capture in 3–5 years given competition and resources

**VC red flag**: Citing TAM without SAM/SOM logic. Show how you've segmented the market, why your beachhead is winnable, and how you step-expand from SOM to SAM.

## 3. Competitive Positioning

### April Dunford's Positioning Methodology
Five components completed in order:

1. **Competitive Alternatives**: "What would the customer do if you didn't exist?" In B2B, often the status quo (do nothing, spreadsheets, build in-house) — defines your real competitive set.
2. **Unique Attributes**: Specific, provable capabilities no alternative offers — named features, architectural choices, operational capabilities, not vague claims like "easy to use."
3. **Value and proof**: What economic, functional, or emotional value does each attribute deliver? Each must map to a defensible claim with evidence (case studies, metrics, benchmarks).
4. **Best-Fit Customers**: Which customers value your attributes most? This defines ICP from a positioning lens.
5. **Market Category**: The frame that orients a new prospect toward your differentiated value. Choosing the wrong category forces you to compete against the wrong alternatives on the wrong criteria.

**Category creation warning**: Companies don't create categories — categories emerge from market conditions. Category creation requires nine-figure revenue runways for market education. Early-stage startups should compete within existing categories with sharper differentiation.

### Narrative Design
Beyond the positioning document — how you tell the story emotionally:
1. **Name the enemy**: The old way that creates pain
2. **The villain**: Why the old way is no longer adequate
3. **The promised land**: The world as it should be
4. **Your magic**: How your product creates that world
5. **Proof**: Evidence you deliver

This structure works simultaneously as sales story, content strategy, and investor narrative.

## 4. Creative Brand Strategy

### How Stripe, Linear, Notion, Figma Built Iconic Brands
All four treated **brand as product strategy, not marketing overhead**. They invested in brand identity before major marketing budgets, understanding that brand is the accumulated residue of user experience.

**Stripe**: Made the developer the hero. Beautiful documentation signaled that this company understood aesthetics as a proxy for technical craft. Stripe Press published writing on economic growth — positioning Stripe as a participant in a larger mission. The brand appeals to developers (peer-to-peer credibility) and enterprise buyers (premium signal) simultaneously.

**Linear**: Confident, precise voice matched to a product obsessed with speed and efficiency. Dark mode by default, keyboard-first design, sparse copy, performance-first messaging — every brand touchpoint reinforces the "sports car, not minivan" thesis.

**Notion**: Minimalist monochromatic identity with hand-drawn illustrations creates warmth in contrast to cold enterprise software. Template gallery is a brand strategy — it shows the community as the product, making the brand feel owned by users.

**Figma**: Bold gradients, expressive illustration, informal tone. FigJam multiplayer experience is a brand statement — design is a team sport. Config conference became a community brand event that galvanized designers around Figma as a movement.

**Common pattern**: (1) Distinctive visual system, (2) Consistent tone anchored in ICP's worldview, (3) Brand story connected to a larger mission, (4) Community ownership — users belong to something, not just use something.

### Tone of Voice Framework
Derive from three inputs:
1. **ICP's vocabulary and worldview** — write how they think, not how you want to be perceived
2. **Your differentiation** — if you're precise and fast, write short sentences with no filler; if collaborative and creative, write warm and invitational
3. **What you are emphatically NOT** — define the anti-tone as clearly as the tone

## 5. Content & Developer GTM

### Vercel's Developer-Led GTM Flywheel
1. **Own the open-source project (Next.js)**: Framework adoption creates millions of developers who use it before encountering Vercel as a product. The framework is the top of funnel.
2. **Deploy with one click on Vercel**: Path from learning Next.js to deploying on Vercel is frictionless. Developers experience the value proposition (instant deployments, preview URLs, edge functions) without a sales interaction.

Result: $200M+ revenue business built on developer trust accumulated through open-source, converting community into commercial motion.

### Supabase's Community-Led Growth
- GitHub as distribution: 70,000+ stars made it a discovery channel
- Weekly releases (changelog as content): Regular shipping signals product velocity and builds trust
- Discord as community infrastructure: 200,000+ members creating peer support at scale
- Community-generated tutorials outpaced company content 10:1 — reducing CAC via ecosystem evangelism

### HashiCorp Open-Core Playbook
1. Build the open-source tool practitioners adopt individually (Terraform, Vault, Consul)
2. Make the open-source version genuinely excellent — do not cripple it
3. When practitioners try to use it at organizational scale, the pain points (audit logs, governance, team workflows) become the enterprise tier's value proposition
4. The internal champion becomes the internal sponsor for the enterprise deal

**Key lesson**: Open-source is a distribution strategy, not a product strategy. The product is the enterprise tier.

## 6. Product Strategy & Prioritization

### RICE, ICE, Kano — When to Use Each

**RICE** (Reach × Impact × Confidence ÷ Effort): Best for comparing competing roadmap initiatives where trade-offs need to be visible and defensible across a team.

**ICE** (Impact × Confidence × Ease): Faster, less overhead. Best for growth experiments and early-stage teams where iteration speed matters more than precision.

**Kano Model** — classifies features:
- *Must-haves (Basic needs)*: Absence causes dissatisfaction; presence neutral. Table stakes.
- *Performance features*: Linear correlation — more = better.
- *Delighters*: Unexpected features that create disproportionate delight. Decay into must-haves over time.
- *Indifferent*: Customers don't care either way.

**Combined framework**: JTBD interviews to identify jobs → Kano surveys to classify features → RICE to prioritize.

### Feature vs. Platform Strategy
**Feature trap**: Building features in response to sales requests without a coherent platform thesis. Results in a product that is a collection of disconnected capabilities, not a system with compounding value.

**Platform play**: Build a system where (1) data generated by one feature improves another, (2) third parties extend via APIs and integrations, (3) customer data creates lock-in increasing switching costs. Salesforce AppExchange: integrations create dependencies making replacement prohibitively expensive.

**Decision rule**: "Does this feature make every other feature better?" If yes, it's a platform investment. If no, evaluate it on RICE terms.

### AI Product Strategy (2025–2026)

**AI-native vs. AI-enhanced:**
- AI-native: AI is the architecture — remove it and the product stops functioning (Cursor, Perplexity, Midjourney)
- AI-enhanced: AI features layered on existing architecture (Salesforce Einstein, Google Docs Smart Compose)
- Strategic implication: McKinsey 2025 — AI-native products generated 2.6x faster revenue growth than AI-enhanced alternatives in the same categories

**Defensibility stack (in priority order for 2026):**
1. **Workflow moat** (most defensible): Deep integration into daily operations makes replacement disruptive — switching requires retraining users, migrating data, rebuilding integrations
2. **Data flywheel** (dynamic, not static): Usage generates feedback → model improvement → more usage → more feedback. Static proprietary datasets are NOT moats — replicable via synthetic data. The feedback loop is the moat.
3. **UX moat**: In an era of commoditizing AI capabilities, interface differentiation is genuine. Linear's UX is its brand and its moat.
4. **Distribution moat**: Existing user base or platform access that new entrants can't replicate
5. **Network effects**: AI output improves as more users contribute
6. **Brand/trust moat**: In regulated industries, trust takes years to establish
7. **Domain expertise moat**: Fine-tuned models on specialized domain data that generalist LLMs can't match

**Key verdict**: "Forget the data moat — the workflow is your fortress" (Vendep, 2025).

## 7. Growth Strategy

### Viral Loops & Network Effects

**Viral loop anatomy**: (1) User experiences value → (2) User invites/shares → (3) New user joins → (4) Cycle repeats. Viral coefficient K = (invitations per user) × (conversion rate). K > 1 = exponential. K < 1 = requires external input. Most companies: K 0.1–0.5, which still meaningfully reduces CAC.

**Network effect taxonomy:**
- **Direct (same-side)**: More users → more value for each (WhatsApp, LinkedIn)
- **Indirect (cross-side)**: More users on one side → more value on other (Uber: drivers ↔ riders)
- **Data network effects**: More usage → better AI/algorithm → better product → more users (Google Search, Spotify)
- **Platform network effects**: More developers → more integrations → more enterprise value (Salesforce, Slack)

**Critical finding**: Viral growth without retention is a leaky bucket. A highly-retained product with K=0.3 will outperform a viral product with K=0.8 and poor retention over 18 months. **Fix retention first, then engineer virality.**

### Activation Funnel (AARRR)
Acquisition → Activation → Retention → Revenue → Referral. Most leveraged intervention for early-stage: **Activation** — the moment a user first experiences core value (the "aha moment").

- Slack aha moment: 2,000 team messages sent
- Dropbox: 1 file stored
- HubSpot: first email send

**Activation optimization toolkit:**
1. Time-to-value reduction — remove every step between sign-up and aha moment
2. Onboarding personalization — route different user types to different aha moments
3. Empty state design — the empty state is the highest-friction moment; fill it with value, not instructions
4. Progress mechanics — show users their progress toward the aha moment

## 8. Creative Problem-Solving Frameworks

### First Principles Thinking
Break every assumption into fundamental truths and rebuild from there. "What do we know is true about this customer problem, independent of how every existing solution has approached it?"

- SpaceX: "What are rockets made of, and what do those materials cost?" (Answer: 2% of rocket price — rest is supply chain inefficiency)
- Uber: "What is the fundamental job of urban transportation?" (not "how do we improve taxis?")

**Startup application**: Most valuable when an industry has long-standing assumptions that incumbents profit from. Identify the assumption, then ask "If we had no legacy constraints, how would we build this?"

### Inversion (Charlie Munger / Jeff Bezos)
"Invert, always invert." Instead of "How do we grow?" ask "What would guarantee our failure, and how do we prevent each of those things?"

Amazon's press release and FAQ process (working backward from a future customer announcement) is inversion applied to product development. In competitive strategy: "What would our most dangerous competitor do to destroy us?" is more generative than "What should we build next?"

### Design Thinking (IDEO / Stanford d.school)
**Empathize → Define → Ideate → Prototype → Test**. Most useful when customer behavior is poorly understood. Critical discipline: do not jump to Ideate before completing Empathize and Define. The majority of premature feature investment comes from skipping to solution before characterizing the problem space.

## 9. Partnership Strategy

### Technology Partnerships
**Partnership types and strategic logic:**
- **Technology integration partners**: Complement your product functionally — both products win (Stripe + Shopify)
- **Channel partners**: Resell or refer to segments you can't reach efficiently; ISVs, consultants, agencies
- **OEM partnerships**: Your technology embedded inside a partner's product — lower margin, massive distribution at zero CAC
- **Strategic ecosystem**: Platform integrations creating bilateral dependency (Salesforce AppExchange, AWS Marketplace, Snowflake Marketplace)

**Win-win structure**: The best partnerships have clear asymmetry. Party with larger distribution takes the integration role; party with better technology takes the partner role. Avoid partnerships where both parties want distribution from each other and neither goes first.

### API Economy as GTM
API-first positions your product as infrastructure inside other products. Stripe's entire early distribution was API-first — developers integrated Stripe into their products, creating millions of endpoints without a direct enterprise sales motion. The developer is the distribution channel.

## 10. International Expansion Strategy

### When to Go Global
Signals: (1) Demonstrable, repeatable unit economics at home, (2) Inbound demand from a specific international market without marketing investment (pull signal), (3) Product is language-independent or you have budget for genuine localization, (4) Home competitive moat is stable enough to divide attention.

**Anti-pattern**: Expanding before achieving SOM dominance at home — dilutes focus, creates operational complexity, fails in both markets.

### Market Selection Priority
Rank markets using: (1) TAM in segment, (2) Cultural proximity (language, business norms, regulatory), (3) Competition intensity, (4) Infrastructure (payments, cloud, developer talent), (5) Regulatory burden.

For SaaS: UK/Canada/Australia are typically first for US startups due to English language, common law, digital infrastructure. For EU: GDPR compliance is prerequisite and differentiator.

### Localization vs. Standardization
**Standardize**: Core product, brand identity, pricing architecture, security/compliance posture.

**Localize**: Language and copy, payment methods (iDEAL in Netherlands, Boleto in Brazil), customer success timezone coverage, regulatory compliance (GDPR, India's DPDP Act, China's PIPL).

## 11. Pivot Strategy

### Eric Ries's Pivot Taxonomy (Lean Startup)
A pivot is a structured course correction testing a new fundamental hypothesis — not iteration (changing feature details) and not a restart (abandoning the company).

**Ten pivot types:**
1. **Zoom-In**: Single feature becomes the whole product (Slack: internal IRC → product)
2. **Zoom-Out**: Whole product becomes a single feature of a larger product
3. **Customer Segment**: Same product, different customer
4. **Customer Need**: Same customer, different problem
5. **Platform**: Application becomes a platform (or vice versa)
6. **Business Architecture**: High-margin/low-volume ↔ low-margin/high-volume
7. **Value Capture**: Change monetization model
8. **Engine of Growth**: Switch from viral to paid to sticky growth engine
9. **Channel**: Same product, different distribution
10. **Technology**: Same customer, same job, different underlying technology

### Famous Pivot Case Studies

| Company | Original | Pivot | Lesson |
|---|---|---|---|
| Instagram | Burbn (check-in app) | Photo sharing only | Zoom-in. Simplicity around one behavior beats feature parity |
| Twitter | Odeo (podcast platform) | 140-char status updates | Constraint (SMS limits) became product identity |
| Slack | Glitch (failed game) | Internal chat tool | Dog-fooding at scale is a discovery mechanism |
| YouTube | Dating video site | Any video | Remove the constraint; let behavior define the product |
| Shopify | Snowboard store | E-commerce infrastructure | The tool you build to solve your own problem is often the product |

### When to Pivot (Signals)
- Declining cohort retention despite product iteration
- Sales cycles getting longer, not shorter
- Same conversations with prospects repeatedly — same objections, same feature gaps
- Engineers are shipping but the key metric isn't moving
- Growing in a sub-segment that differs from your assumed ICP

### When NOT to Pivot
- You're impatient, not learning
- Cohort retention is improving even if top-line is slow
- You haven't given your current hypothesis enough time to generate learnable data

## 12. Integrated Startup Strategy Mental Model

When advising, apply this decision sequence:

1. **Discover the job** (JTBD interviews) before building anything
2. **Build the minimum that delivers the job** and measure PMF (Sean Ellis + retention cohorts)
3. **Identify your true ICP** from the "most disappointed" segment
4. **Position against real competitive alternatives** (Dunford's 5 components); resist category creation until you have the revenue runway to fund market education
5. **Choose GTM motion** based on deal size, buyer complexity, and time-to-value — default to hybrid PLG+SLG above $2M ARR
6. **Build brand as product strategy** — tone, visual identity, and narrative design from day one, anchored in your ICP's worldview
7. **Engineer growth loops** with retention as the prerequisite — viral mechanics on a leaky bucket will not compound
8. **Build AI defensibility via workflow integration and data flywheels**, not static data accumulation
9. **Cross the chasm** by choosing a beachhead niche small enough to dominate fully before expanding
10. **Pivot on validated learning**, not impatience — use Ries's taxonomy to name the specific hypothesis being changed

## Advisor Behavior

When advising:
1. **Ask what stage they're at first** before recommending frameworks — pre-PMF founders need different advice than Series A CTOs
2. **Be opinionated**: Give a specific recommendation with tradeoffs stated, not a survey of all options
3. **Use the right framework for the question**: Don't apply Wardley Mapping to a tactical GTM question or RICE to a positioning question
4. **Name the enemy**: Always help identify what the startup is positioning *against* (competitive alternative or status quo), not just what they're positioning for
5. **Flag premature scaling**: If a founder wants to expand internationally or launch multiple products before achieving strong PMF in their core market, name this as a risk
6. **Distinguish signal from noise in PMF**: A PMF survey is not evidence on its own — always triangulate with retention data and qualitative interviews
