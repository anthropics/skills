# Trademark Law for Technology Startups

## What Trademark Protects

A trademark is any word, name, symbol, device, or combination thereof that identifies and distinguishes the source of goods or services. Trademarks protect brand identity — not inventions (patent) or creative expression (copyright).

**For tech startups**: Company name, product names, logos, taglines, distinctive UI elements (trade dress), and app names can all be trademarks.

**Federal trademark registration (USPTO)** provides:
- Nationwide constructive notice of ownership
- Legal presumption of validity and ownership
- Ability to use ® symbol
- Basis for domain name dispute resolution (UDRP)
- Basis for international trademark applications (Madrid Protocol)
- Enhanced remedies including up to treble damages for willful infringement

**Common law trademark** arises automatically from actual use in commerce. No registration required. But protection is geographically limited to the area of actual use and is harder to enforce.

## International Classification for Tech Companies

The **Nice Agreement** classifies goods and services into 45 classes. Tech companies typically file in:

**International Class 9 (IC 009)** — Downloadable software, apps, hardware:
- Downloadable computer software
- Mobile applications
- Computer hardware
- Electronic devices
- SaaS tools distributed as downloadable software (vs. cloud services)

**International Class 42 (IC 042)** — Software-as-a-Service, tech services, SaaS:
- Computer programming and software development services
- Cloud computing services (SaaS, PaaS, IaaS)
- Software-as-a-service (SaaS)
- Technology consulting and IT services
- Artificial intelligence services
- Research and development in technology fields
- Cybersecurity services

**Additional classes commonly needed by tech startups**:
- IC 035 — Business services, advertising, online marketplace platforms
- IC 038 — Telecommunications, streaming, communication platforms
- IC 045 — Legal and security services (for compliance/identity products)
- IC 041 — Education and entertainment services (for e-learning, gaming)

**Filing strategy**: Most SaaS startups need IC 042 at minimum. If you distribute downloadable software or apps, add IC 009. Each class requires a separate filing fee ($250–$350 per class on TEAS Plus).

## The USPTO Trademark Registration Process

### Step 1: Trademark Clearance Search
Before filing, conduct a comprehensive clearance search:
- **USPTO TESS (Trademark Electronic Search System)**: Search registered and pending marks
- **Common law search**: Google, domain registrations, social media, app stores — unregistered marks can still block registration
- **Professional search**: Trademark attorneys use Thomson CompuMark, Corsearch, or similar databases for comprehensive full clearance searches
- Cost: $500–$2,500 for attorney-conducted clearance search; $2,000–$5,000 for full professional clearance

**Why this matters**: Filing a mark that conflicts with an existing registration wastes the $250–$350 filing fee, triggers expensive opposition proceedings, and may require rebranding.

### Step 2: Filing the Application (TEAS)
File through the Trademark Electronic Application System (TEAS):
- **TEAS Plus**: $250 per class — must use pre-approved identification of goods/services
- **TEAS Standard**: $350 per class — allows custom description of goods/services

**Use in Commerce vs. Intent-to-Use**:
- **Use in Commerce**: Mark already being used in interstate commerce. More straightforward.
- **Intent-to-Use (ITU)**: Mark not yet in use. Establishes priority date. Must file a Statement of Use or extension requests within 6 months of Notice of Allowance (extendable up to 3 years total, at additional cost)

### Step 3: USPTO Examination (3-6 months)
USPTO examiner reviews for:
- Likelihood of confusion with existing marks (most common rejection)
- Descriptiveness (marks that merely describe the goods/services are rejected)
- Geographic terms, surnames, and functional features
- Immoral or scandalous content

**If refused**: Applicant receives an Office Action and must respond within 3 months (extendable to 6 months).

### Step 4: Publication for Opposition (30 days)
If approved, the mark is published in the Official Gazette. Third parties have 30 days to file an opposition (extendable by 90 days on request). Opposition proceedings before the TTAB (Trademark Trial and Appeal Board) can take 1–3+ years.

### Step 5: Registration
If no opposition is filed (or opposition is resolved in applicant's favor):
- For use-based applications: Certificate of Registration issued
- For ITU applications: Notice of Allowance issued; applicant must submit Statement of Use

**Total timeline**: 8–18 months typical; can extend 2+ years with opposition proceedings.
**Total cost**: $1,500–$5,000 for attorney-assisted registration (single class); more for multi-class filings.

## Domain Names and Trademark

### UDRP (Uniform Domain Name Dispute Resolution Policy)
The **ICANN UDRP** provides an expedited administrative proceeding to transfer cybersquatted domain names. To prevail, the complainant must prove three elements:
1. Domain is identical or confusingly similar to a trademark the complainant has rights in
2. The registrant has no rights or legitimate interests in the domain
3. The domain was registered and is being used in bad faith

**2024 statistics**: 6,168 UDRP cases filed from 133 countries. Over 95% of recent decisions resulted in transfer to the trademark owner. Average cost: $1,500–$4,000 in attorney fees. Proceedings conclude in ~2 months.

**Anti-Cybersquatting Consumer Protection Act (ACPA)**: US federal law (15 U.S.C. § 1125(d)) allows trademark owners to sue cybersquatters in federal court. Statutory damages of $1,000–$100,000 per domain name. Can be used when UDRP is unavailable or the registrant is US-based.

**Practical tips**:
- Register your brand name domain BEFORE filing the trademark application
- Register common typos, plurals, and alternate extensions (.net, .io, .co) defensively
- Monitor new domain registrations using services like MarkMonitor

### App Store Trademark Issues
App store trademark disputes are an evolving area. Apple and Google may reject app names that infringe existing trademarks. Platform-level decisions (removal from app store) are separate from USPTO trademark rights. File for trademark protection early to establish priority — useful in disputes with competing app names.

## International Trademark Strategy

### Madrid Protocol (International Registration System)
The Madrid Protocol allows a single international application to designate protection in 130+ member countries, based on a "home" registration or application.

**Process**:
1. File or have a pending/registered trademark in the home country (US)
2. File an international application with USPTO (as the Office of Origin)
3. WIPO examines formalities and transmits to designated countries
4. Each designated country examines under its own national law

**Costs** (approximate):
- WIPO basic fee: ~$653 CHF (~$720 USD) plus per-country fees
- US designation: ~$500 per class
- EU designation: ~$920 for first class, $165 for each additional class
- Total for US + EU + UK + major markets: $3,000–$8,000+ depending on classes and countries

**Timeline**: 12–18 months from filing to registrations in most jurisdictions.

### Direct EUIPO Filing (EU Trade Mark)
- Single application covers all 27 EU member states
- Cost: ~€1,350 for one class; ~€1,500 for two classes online
- Provides unitary protection across entire EU
- May be preferable to Madrid when EU coverage is the primary goal

### Key International Differences
- **China**: First-to-file system (vs. first-to-use in US). File in China early — before entering the Chinese market — to prevent squatting. Classes in China are subdivided into sub-categories.
- **EU**: Broader scope of protection; bad faith filings challenged more easily
- **India**: 4-5 year backlog; file early if India is a target market
- **UK**: Post-Brexit, EU trademark no longer covers UK; separate UK filing needed

## Trademark Strength Spectrum

From strongest (most protectable) to weakest:

1. **Fanciful**: Invented words with no prior meaning (Kodak, Xerox, Zoom) — strongest
2. **Arbitrary**: Real words used in unrelated context (Apple for computers, Amazon for marketplace) — very strong
3. **Suggestive**: Hints at qualities without describing them (Netflix suggests streaming movies) — protectable
4. **Descriptive**: Describes a feature or characteristic (Fast Computers) — NOT inherently protectable; can acquire "secondary meaning" over time
5. **Generic**: The common name for the product or service (Computer for computers) — never protectable; genericness kills a trademark

**Common mistake for tech startups**: Choosing descriptive names like "Quick Analytics" or "AI Writer" that face USPTO descriptiveness rejections and are difficult to protect.
